# Efficient Shapley values computation for Boolean network models of gene regulation 

Giang Pham, Silvia Giulia Galfr` e and Paolo Milazzo 

Abstract —Identifying dynamically influential nodes in biological networks is a central problem in systems biology, particularly for prioritizing intervention tar-gets in gene regulatory networks. In this paper, we propose a Shapley-value-based framework for assess-ing the importance of nodes in a Boolean network with respect to a given target node. The framework comprises two complementary measures: the Knock-out and the Knock-in Shapley values . Moreover, we present a propagation-based method that enables their efficient computation. By exploiting the logical structure of the network, the method avoids exhaus-tive simulations. The approach is exact for acyclic networks and provides good approximations for cyclic networks. Evaluation on benchmark models from the Cell Collective database shows that the propagation method accurately recovers node importance rankings while achieving substantial speed-ups. 

Index Terms —Boolean networks, node importance, Knock-out, Shapley value, network propagation. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Boolean networks (BNs), introduced by Stu-art Kauffman [1], have become a widely adopted framework for modeling gene regulatory networks (GRNs). Their success is largely due to their con-ceptual simplicity and their ability to qualitatively capture regulatory interactions among genes, while still preserving essential dynamical features. As a result, BNs have been successfully applied to a broad range of biological systems and processes [2], [3], [4]. A central application of BN modeling is the identification of potential intervention targets [5], which naturally leads to the problem of quantifying the importance or influence of individual nodes within the network. Early efforts in this direction focused on char-acterizing node influence at the level of Boolean functions. In particular, Shmulevich and Kauffman introduced the notion of activity [6], which mea-sures the influence of a variable on the output of a Boolean function. For a Boolean function f (x) with 

x = ( x1, x 2, . . . , x k), the activity of a variable xj

is defined as the average of the partial derivative of 

f with respect to xj over all possible configurations 

x ∈ { 0, 1}k. This concept was further analyzed in the context of canalizing functions, and it was shown to have a direct impact on the dynamical behavior of BNs. While foundational, this line of work primarily emphasizes mathematical properties of Boolean functions rather than applications to large-scale biological networks. Ghanbarnejad and Klemm addressed this limita-tion by extending the notion of variable activity to quantify node influence in BN dynamics [7]. They defined the dynamical impact of a node as the fraction of all possible state combinations for which a perturbation of that node propagates through the network within a given time horizon. This dynam-ical impact can be approximated by the principal eigenvector of the activity matrix, where each entry 

αij quantifies the influence of node i on node j

(i.e., the activity of i on the Boolean function fj ,as defined in earlier work by Shmulevich and Kauff-man). Applied to a fibroblast network, the method correctly identified four of the five nodes with the highest dynamical impact over 100 time steps. De-spite its empirical effectiveness, the approach is not genuinely time-resolved, as the eigenvector contains no explicit temporal information [8]. Moreover, it assumes that all state combinations occur with equal probability [8]. Subsequent studies shifted toward information-theoretic perspectives that directly address network-

level behavior. In 2013, Heckel et al. introduced the concept of determinative power (DP) [9] of an input node, defined via mutual information between the state of that input and the states of all non-input nodes in the network. Applied to the E. coli regulatory network, DP enabled the identifi-cation of a small subset of input nodes capable of substantially reducing uncertainty in the global network state. However, this framework was limited to networks with tree-like topologies, assumed the absence of input dependencies and feedback loops. It also restricts DP to input nodes only. These constraints were later relaxed by Matache 

et al. , who extended the notion of DP to general nodes while relaxing the assumption of indepen-dence among the inputs of a Boolean function, which allows the method to work with general BNs [10]. In addition, they introduced the concept of 

Strength , which quantifies the causal influence of a node on others under perturbation. Their results highlighted mutual information as a powerful tool for identifying subsets of relevant nodes that max-imize information gain, and they demonstrated the applicability of these ideas in a case study on a fibroblast network. Further analysis by Pentzien et al. showed that DP is weakly correlated with simple topological features such as the number of outgoing edges, but is independent of classical centrality measures including closeness and betweenness centrality [11]. Importantly, DP was shown to recover biologi-cally essential nodes. This notion is a theoreti-cally grounded, information-based measure, but it requires either the assumption on the independence among input nodes of Boolean functions as in the work of Heckel et al. or the extensive information about the probability of each state as in the relaxed version of Matache et al. In addition, it is a generic network measure and is not tailored to specific phenotypic markers. In parallel, other approaches have sought to identify dynamically influential nodes using purely static network properties. In this context, determi-native power has been combined with vertex be-tweenness to identify nodes that are most effective in altering network dynamics [5], [12]. Additional analyses incorporating static features such as con-nectivity and hub structure led to the identification of a class of low-degree nodes, termed gatekeepers ,which constitute promising targets for drug discov-ery and the identification of disease drivers [12]. Despite their differences, the approaches de-scribed above share a common aim: identifying nodes with strong dynamical impact in Boolean networks while avoiding computationally expensive simulations, typically by relying on mathematical abstractions that assume a product distribution or equal probability across all possible states. In con-trast, we recently addressed this problem more directly by introducing the Knock-out Shapley value 

[13], [14], which quantifies node importance based on simulation results from systematic knock-out ex-periments. This approach is conceptually intuitive, as it closely mirrors experimental gene knock-out studies, and its biological relevance was demon-strated on CD4 + T cell models [14]. However, this fidelity comes at a substantial computational cost, as the number of required simulations grows rapidly, with the complexity of each simulation increasing exponentially with the number of input nodes. In this paper we propose a more general Shapley-value-based framework consisting of two measures: the Knock-out Shapley value and the Knock-in Shapley value . Moreover, in order to overcome the limitations related to computational complexity, we introduce a propagation-based method that enables the efficient computation of both measures without performing the knock-out/knock-in simulations. The Knock-out and Knock-in Shapley values enable the systematic analysis of gene influence in GRNs across varying environmental conditions. Within this framework, the marginal contribution of each gene to the presence of a given target node is evaluated across multiple input configurations. Averaging these contributions yields a quantitative measure of the influence of individual genes on the resulting phenotypic outcome. This provides a principled assessment of gene importance with respect to a specific target. Although these notions are conceptually mean-ingful, their standard computation is computa-tionally expensive and does not scale to large 3

Boolean models [14]. The proposed propagation-based method addresses this limitation by enabling efficient and scalable estimation, thereby making large-scale screening analyses feasible. The propa-gation method successfully recovers node rankings in most benchmark models, achieving a normalized discounted cumulative gain (NDCG) of 0.75 or higher. Moreover, the method improves compu-tational efficiency, reducing the time complexity from O(( n + m)2 × 2n) to O(( n + m) × 2n).An implementation of the proposed method, to-gether with scripts to reproduce the experiments, is publicly available at https://github.com/giangpth/ ShapleyValueForBooleanNetwork.git. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the necessary back-ground on Boolean networks and the Shapley value. Section III presents the Knock-out, Knock-in Shap-ley value and the proposed propagation-based com-putation method, together with its correctness for acyclic networks and a complexity analysis. Sec-tion IV evaluates the propagation approach by com-paring its results with direct simulation and further contrasts our method with other notions through two case studies. Finally, Section V concludes the paper and outlines future work. II. B ACKGROUND

2) Propagation for Boolean operators: Since the computation of the Knock-in and Knock-out Shapley values can be reformulated as the problem of identifying rows in which flipping the value of a node alters the value of the target node, we begin the procedure at the target node T. For the target node, all rows are relevant, as any change in its value trivially affects its own state. Starting from T, the set of relevant rows is then propagated upward through the network until the input nodes are reached. Because the network under consideration is a BBN, each node depends on at most two other nodes, or equivalently, has at most two incoming edges in the graphical representation. This structural constraint allows the propagation rules to be defined in a simple and systematic manner. 

a) OR operator: A = B OR C : For a binary 

OR operator of the form A = B OR C , changing the value of B affects the value of A only when 

C = 0 , since A = 1 whenever C = 1 , regardless of the value of B. Consequently, among the rows associated with node A, only those rows for which 

C = 0 are propagated to node B, and analogously, rows with B = 0 are propagated to node C.As an illustrative example, consider the net-work shown in Figure 2. For the OR operator

3) Non-trivial structures: A BBN may contain non-trivial structures that are not captured by the proposed Boolean operator rules. In particular, they are diamond-shaped convergences and cycles, in which cycles require approximations in the prop-agation results. 

a) Diamond structures: Beyond simple unary and binary operator structures, a BBN may also contain diamond-shaped motifs, as illustrated in Figure 3a (with I functions as target node), in which a node B influences a downstream node G

through two distinct incoming paths via nodes D

and E. In such configurations, the propagation rules defined for unary or binary operators are no longer directly applicable because D and E are no longer independent. Indeed, the set of rows associated with node B must first be a subset of the rows associated with node G, since B influences the target node I

through G. However, because the value of G depends on both B (via nodes D and E) and A (via node 

D), not all rows associated with G contribute to the influence of B.To address this case, a straightforward approach is to partially simulate the diamond structure, which in this example involves the four nodes B, D, E,and G. To identify the rows associated with B,we simulate flipping the value of B in each row associated with G and retain only those rows for which this perturbation leads to a change in G.In a general BBN, diamond structures can be non-trivial because nodes involved in cycles may induce self-influencing diamonds, as illustrated in Figure 3b. In this example, in addition to the diamond formed by A, B, C, and D, there is another diamond originating from D, passing through A, B

in one side, and C in the other side, and feeding back into D. Such configurations significantly com-plicate the treatment of diamond motifs. Therefore, we restrict the diamond motifs to acyclic BBNs. More precisely, we identify diamond structures on an acyclic approximation of the original network obtained by removing all cycles. The details of this procedure are discussed in Section III-E. Removing cycles prior to diamond identification may cause some diamond structures to be missed, constituting the first source of approximation in our propagation method.

C. Correctness in acyclic networks 

Since all approximations arise from cycles in the BBN, the propagation method is exact for acyclic networks. This is first validated empirically on synthetic acyclic networks (see Supplementary, Fig-ure S9). We now formally establish the correctness of the propagation method in the acyclic case. The complete proof is provided in the Supplementary; here, we present a sketch of the argument. 

Theorem 1. Let Bn,m be an acyclic binarized Boolean network and let T be a target node. For any node X, the knockout and knockin Shapley values computed by the proposed propagation method are equal to those obtained by explicit simulation of 

Bn,m .9

Proof. Restrict to the backward reachable subgraph 

BT from target node T, since nodes outside it have zero Shapley value. Because the graph is acyclic, 

BT admits a topological order toward T, and T

can be written as a Boolean formula obtained by composing node functions along this order. Each propagated row corresponds to a coalition. At T, propagation enumerates the truth table and counts exactly the coalitions that flip the output under knock-in/knock-out, matching the Shapley definitions. Assume correctness for all nodes closer to T. For a node Y, two cases: (i) If Y starts a diamond, partial simulation en-sures each coalition affecting T is counted for Y, so contributions match explicit simulation. (ii) Otherwise, all paths from Y go through a unique successor X; changing Y affects T iff it af-fects X. Since X is correct and propagation computes Boolean updates correctly, Y is also correct. By induction, propagation matches explicit simu-lation on all nodes in BT , completing the proof. 

D. Complexity analysis 

Consider a Boolean network Bn,m with n nodes, among which m are input nodes. The remaining n−

m nodes are internal nodes associated with Boolean update functions. 

Complexity of the standard simulation: In the worst-case scenario, identifying the attractor land-scape of a Boolean network requires a traversal of the complete state transition graph. While the state space consists of 2n vertices, the cost of computing the successor of any given state is proportional to the number of non-input nodes. Consequently, the algorithmic complexity for a brute-force simulation is O(( n − m)2 n), where n − m represents the set of internal nodes subject to regulatory update rules [18]. In the standard analysis, the impact of each internal node is evaluated through a knock-out/knock-in experiment. This requires performing one simulation for each of the n−m internal nodes. Consequently, the total computational cost becomes 

O(( n − m)22n).

Size of the binarized Boolean network: Let the original network be Bn,m = (V, E ), where 

|V | = n. After binarization, the resulting binarized Boolean network Bbn,m therefore contains |V b| =

|V | + O(|E|) vertices and |Eb| = O(|V | + |E|)

edges. 

Cost of the propagation method: The com-putational cost of the propagation method consists of two components: (i) traversing the BBN, and (ii) simulating diamond structures. Traversing the network requires O(|V | + |E|) time. Two factors influence the computational cost as-sociated with diamond structures are:(i) the fraction of diamonds in the network; (ii) the number of rows associated with the ending node of each diamond. Let ♢ (♢ < 1) denote the fraction of nodes that correspond to the beginning of a diamond structure. In the worst case, the number or rows needed to simulate for a diamond is 2m. It is also important to node that diamond simulation corresponds to updating the values of nodes involved in these struc-tures and is distinct from the simulation required to identify attractors in the state transition graph. Taking into account the number of diamonds and the cost of processing them, the complexity of the propagation is O (|V | + |E|)2♢ 2m.

Overall complexity: Combining the simula-tion of the binary network to build the truth table and the propagation, the overall run-ning time of the method can be expressed as 

O (|V | + |E| − m)2 |V |+|E| + ( |V | + |E|)2♢ 2m.Since the first term grows exponentially with the size of the binarized network, it dominates the total complexity. Therefore, the overall worst-case complexity is O(( |V | + |E| − m)2 |V |+|E|).GRNs are typically sparse, with the number of regulatory interactions growing approximately lin-early with the number of genes. Empirical studies indicate that the number of edges |E| scales with the number of nodes n as |E| = O(n), leading to a small and approximately constant average degree 

⟨k⟩ = |E|/n across networks of different sizes [19], [20], [21]. The overall complexity can be written as 

O(( n − m)2 n). Compared to the standard analysis, the propagation method removes one multiplicative factor of (n − m), yielding a linear reduction in computational cost. This improvement becomes especially relevant for large-scale networks with a substantial number of internal nodes. 10 

E. Implementation details 

Our propagation rules are defined for BBNs. We therefore first transform a general Boolean network into an equivalent BBN in which (i) each node has at most two inputs, and (ii) any node with a neg-atively influential input has exactly one incoming edge. This transformation preserves the simulation behavior of the original network. To identify diamond structures, the network must be acyclic. We therefore remove a feedback arc set (FAS) from the original network, as it is smaller than the binarized one, and then binarize the re-sulting acyclic network to obtain an approximate acyclic BBN. Diamond structures are detected on this acyclic BBN. We detect the FAS in the original network using Algorithm S1 (Supplementary) of Eades, Lin, and Smyth [22]. 

a) “Binarization”: We binarize the network by introducing intermediate nodes so that each node has at most two incoming edges while pre-serving functionality. For a node A with operands 

I1, I2, I3, an intermediate node I23 aggregates I2

and I3. Node A then receives inputs from I1 and 

I23 . Boolean functions are updated accordingly, e.g., I23 = I2 OR I3 and A = I1 OR I 23 .To preserve synchronous dynamics, all intermediate nodes are updated before the original nodes. 

b) Identification of diamond structures: We detect diamond-like convergence structures by traversing the BBN backward from a designated output node. Each node propagates a carry-on map indicating whether upstream nodes are reached via the left or right branch of binary operators. When multiple paths converge, conflicts in the carry-on states indicate potential diamond closures; nodes with multiple outgoing edges are recorded as sinks. Unary operators propagate the carry-on informa-tion unchanged. The full procedure is given in Supplementary Algorithm S2. Among the detected structures, we select the deepest (most downstream) reconvergence node as the “master diamond” and discard nested smaller diamonds, since simulating the master diamonds is sufficient to determine the corresponding set of rows for the initial node. 

c) Propagation in the Binarized Boolean Net-works: The resulted BBN is still acyclic as the FAS had been removed before the binarization. We reintroduce the removed arcs back to the BBN before performing the propagation. During the prop-agation, the rules in Section III-B are applied as presented in Algorithm S3 (see Supplementary). Besides the BBN, master diamonds, target node, and Boolean formulas, the algorithm takes two indices derived from the truth table: an index I,mapping each node to rows where it evaluates to true, and a complementary index ¯I, mapping to rows where it evaluates to false. These inverted indices enable efficient row queries. Starting from the target node T, the algorithm propagates layer by layer, computing for each node 

X the set of truth-table rows R(X) determining its Shapley values. For nodes with two parents, candidate rows are computed using the AND/OR 

rules in Section III-B and the indices I and ¯I.IV. E VALUATION OF THE PROPAGATION METHOD 

We evaluated the proposed propagation method using a benchmark of 20 models (18 cyclic and 2 acyclic) obtained from the Cell Collective database 2. Models were randomly selected under the constraint that they contain between two and fifteen input nodes. Models exceeding this range were excluded due to their higher computational demands for the baseline simulations, as the exper-iments involve numerous repeated runs. For each model, three target nodes were chosen. Whenever possible, these targets were selected according to the model descriptions (e.g., their biological func-tions); otherwise, they were randomly chosen from nodes sufficiently distant from the input nodes. This selection strategy prevents the inclusion of targets too close to the input layer, which could otherwise bias the estimation of propagation time. The prop-agation results are evaluated against a simulation-based baseline.

C. Further improvement 

Random simulations can improve accuracy in cyclic networks by explicitly simulating a subset of nodes during propagation instead of applying the rules in Section III-B. Experiments (five runs per model-target pair; Figures S1–S2) show that both runtime and the fraction of correctly propagated rows increase roughly linearly with the proportion of simulated nodes, with runtime growing faster. This reduces efficiency but is beneficial when higher accuracy is required. Figure S3 shows that the fraction of correctly propagated rows at input nodes correlates with that across all nodes. Since exact rows at input nodes can be obtained from intact network simulations, they provide a reference to estimate overall propagation error.

ues in BNs. By exploiting the logical structure of binarized networks, the method avoids exhaustive simulations while preserving target-specific impor-tance semantics. Experiments on benchmark models show that the approach recovers node rankings with good accuracy and achieves substantial speed-ups, particularly in networks with many inputs. Despite limitations in cyclic networks, the method provides a practical and scalable alternative to simulation-based Shapley analysis for BN models. Several directions can be taken to strengthen the proposed framework. First, the handling of diamond structures can be further optimized. Instead of par-tially simulating entire diamond subgraphs, future work will focus on detecting the precise entry and exit points of signal interaction within diamonds and on characterizing their logical influence patterns analytically. By examining the conditions under which signals from multiple paths actually interact at the reconvergence node, it should be possible to reduce or eliminate local simulations, thereby improving runtime. Secondly, we will address cyclic networks by 14 

deriving explicit error bounds for propagation in the presence of feedback. By quantifying how cycles distort independence assumptions and by relating propagation errors to structural properties of the cycles (e.g., length, connectivity, or feedback strength), we aim to provide guarantees on the maximum deviation from exact Shapley values. S4: C ORRECTNESS IN ACYCLIC NETWORKS 

Lemma 1 (Row-coalition correspondence) . For a binarized Boolean network Bn,m with input set M =

{x1, . . . , x m}, each propagated row corresponds bijectively to a coalition S ⊆ M . Moreover, the weight associated to the row by the propagation method is the Shapley weight wS of the corresponding coalition. Proof. Each row represents a unique assignment of the input nodes, which corresponds to a unique coalition S of input variables taking value 1. The propagation method preserves this correspondence and does not alter the associated weight. 

Lemma 2 (Unique-successor property) . Let Bn,m be an acyclic Boolean network and let Y be a node reachable from the target node T. If Y is not the beginning of a diamond structure, then Y has a unique immediate successor X on all directed paths from Y to T. In particular, every directed path from Y to T

passes through the same direct child X of Y.Proof. Assume for contradiction that there exist two directed paths from Y to T whose first edges go to two distinct children X1̸ = X2 of Y. Since the network is acyclic and both paths reach T, the subpaths from X1 and X2 must eventually merge at some node Z (possibly Z=T ) before reaching T. Then Y, X1, X2,

and Z form a diamond structure with Y as the beginning node, contradicting the assumption. Hence, all directed paths from Y to T must pass through a unique child X.

Lemma 3 (Influence preservation of bottleneck node) . Let Bn,m be an acyclic binarized Boolean network with target node T . Let Y be a node such that every directed path from Y to T passes through a unique successor X (bottleneck node). Then, for any coalition S ⊆ M , changing the value of Y in wS (row corresponding to coalition S) changes the value of T if and only if changing the value of X in wS

changes the value of T .Proof. Since all directed paths from Y to T pass through X, the value of T depends on Y only through its effect on X. Therefore, for any coalition S, toggling Y changes T if and only if it causes a change in X that propagates to T. By the induction hypothesis, the propagation to X correctly identifies all such coalitions. Propagation from X to Y preserves this correspondence without duplication, since no alternative path exists. Hence each marginal contribution of Y to T is counted exactly once. 

Lemma 4 (Correctness of partial simulation in diamond structures) . Consider a diamond structure in an acyclic Boolean network, where a node Y influences a downstream node Z through multiple directed paths. The partial simulation performed at Y ensures that each row affecting Z contributes exactly once to the knockout or knockin Shapley value with respect to the target node T.Proof. Partial simulation explicitly evaluates the Boolean function at the convergence node Z when the value of the upstream node Y is perturbed. Since Z is the bottleneck for all paths from Y to the target node T, Lemma 3 and Lemma 1 together imply that this partial simulation yields the correct Shapley values for T.

Lemma 5 (Local correctness of propagation) . Let X be a node which is an operand in a binary Boolean operator AND , OR , or unary Boolean operator NOT or identical. For any propagated row reaching X, the propagation rules compute the same value of X as explicit evaluation of its Boolean function under the corresponding coalition. Proof. Since the network is acyclic, child nodes do not influence their parents. The propagation rules therefore exactly implement the truth tables of the AND , OR , NOT , and identity operators when their operands are independent, yielding results identical to explicit simulation. 24 

If, in a binary operator C = A x B, one operand A depends on the other operand B, there exist at least two reconverging paths from C to B (a direct path and one via A). In this case, B is treated as part of a diamond structure and handled correctly by the dedicated simulation procedure (Lemma 4). The other operand A, under knockout or knockin perturbations, acts independently of B, and the propagation rules applied to A remain correct. Hence, in all cases, the propagation rules for Boolean operators are applied correctly. 

Theorem 2. Let Bn,m be an acyclic binarized Boolean network and let T be a target node. For any node 

X, the knockout and knockin Shapley values computed by the proposed propagation method are equal to those obtained by explicit simulation of Bn,m .Proof. Let BT denote the subgraph induced by all nodes that can reach the target node T via directed paths. Nodes outside BT do not influence T; hence, under both explicit simulation and the propagation method, their knockout and knockin Shapley values are zero. We therefore restrict attention to BT .Since Bn,m is acyclic, BT is also acyclic and admits a topological ordering consistent with the direction of information flow towards T. Moreover, T can be expressed as a Boolean formula obtained by successive substitution of Boolean functions along this ordering. Each propagated row corresponds bijectively to a coalition S ⊆ M , with associated weight wS (Lemma 1, Supplementary). 

Base case. At the target node T, the propagation method enumerates all rows of the truth table. Rows for which T = 0 and switches to 1 under Knock-in contribute to the Knock-in Shapley value, while rows for which T = 1 and switches to 0 under Knock-out contribute to the Knock-out Shapley value. This exactly matches the definitions in Equations 8 and 6. Hence, the propagation method is correct at T.

Induction hypothesis. Assume that for all nodes that are topologically closer to T than a given node 

Y, the propagation method computes knock-out and knock-in Shapley values equal to those obtained by explicit simulation. 

Induction step. Let Y be a node in BT whose immediate successors satisfy the induction hypothesis. We consider two cases. 

(i) Y is the beginning of a diamond structure. In this case, Y influences a downstream node through multiple directed paths that later merge. By Lemma 4 (Supplementary), the partial simulation performed at 

Y ensures that each coalition whose row affects the target node contributes exactly once to the knock-out or knock-in Shapley value of Y with respect to T. Since downstream nodes are handled correctly by the induction hypothesis, the propagated values at Y coincide with those obtained by explicit simulation. 

(ii) Y is not the beginning of a diamond structure. By Lemma 2 (Supplementary), every directed path from Y to T passes through a unique immediate successor X. For any coalition S, changing the value of Y

affects T if and only if it affects X (Lemma 3, Supplementary). By the induction hypothesis, propagation computes the correct effect of X on T. Furthermore, the propagation rules for Boolean operators compute the correct value of Y for each row (Lemma 5, Supplementary). Therefore, the propagated knockout and knockin contributions of Y match those obtained by explicit simulation. In both cases, the propagation method is correct for Y. By induction along the topological order of BT ,the propagation method computes correct knockout and knockin Shapley values for all nodes reachable from T. Combined with the fact that all other nodes have zero contribution, this completes the proof.
