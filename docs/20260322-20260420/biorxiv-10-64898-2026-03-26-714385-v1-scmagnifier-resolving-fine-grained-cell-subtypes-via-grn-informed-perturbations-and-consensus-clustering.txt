Abstract 9

Resolving fine-grained cell subtypes in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data 10 

remains challenging, as their subtle transcriptional differences are often obscured by 11 

technical noise and data sparsity. Here, we present scMagnifier, a consensus 12 

clustering framework that leverages gene regulatory network (GRN)-informed in silico 13 

perturbations to amplify subtle transcriptional differences and uncover latent cell 14 

subpopulations. scMagnifier perturbs candidate transcription factors (TFs), propagates 15 

perturbation effects through cluster-specific GRNs to simulate post-perturbation 16 

expression profiles, and integrates clustering results across multiple perturbations into 17 

stable subtype assignments. Additionally, scMagnifier introduces regulatory 18 

perturbation consensus UMAP (rpcUMAP), a perturbation-aware visualization that 19 

provides clearer separation between cell subtypes and guides the selection of the 20 

optimal number of clusters. In both single-batch and multi-batch benchmarks, 21 

scMagnifier consistently improves the resolution and accuracy of fine-grained cell type 22 

identification. Notably, when integrated with spatial clustering methods such as 23 

STAGATE, scMagnifier is compatible with spatial transcriptomics workflows and 24 

effectively reveals tumor cell subtypes and their spatial organization in ovarian cancer. 25

Introduction 27 

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has revolutionized our understanding of 28 

cellular heterogeneity by enabling transcriptome-wide profiling at single-cell resolution 1.29 

Unsupervised clustering is a standard and essential step in the scRNA-seq analysis 30 

workflow, routinely used to identify discrete cell types and continuous cell states 2,3 .31 

With the rapid development of clustering algorithms, current tools can consistently and 32 

accurately resolve major cell types—such as T and B lymphocytes in immune tissues, 33 

neurons and glia in the brain, or epithelial and stromal cells in solid tumors—across a 34 

wide range of biological settings. However, resolving fine-grained cell subtypes and 35 

accurately delineating their boundaries remains challenging—particularly for 36 

transcriptionally similar cell states such as activated versus resting immune cells, 37 

malignant epithelial subclones within tumors, or rare cell populations 2,3 . The high 38 

dimensionality, sparsity, and noise inherent in scRNA-seq data often obscure subtle 39 

but biologically meaningful transcriptional differences, limiting the resolution of 40 

clustering methods for fine-grained heterogeneity 4.41 

Notably, transcriptionally similar cell populations may respond differently to 42 

regulatory perturbations due to variations in their underlying gene regulatory networks 43 

(GRNs), offering a powerful means to uncover hidden heterogeneity 5,6 . Several studies 44 

have employed in silico gene perturbations to simulate key biological processes, such 45 

as cell fate transitions or drug-induced state changes. For example, CellOracle 46 

simulates the effects of transcription factor (TF) perturbations on cell identity dynamics 47 

by modeling GRN influences, providing interpretable and quantitative insights into 48 

developmental trajectories 7. Similarly, scRank perturbs inferred GRNs to simulate 49 

drug-induced regulatory changes, enabling the identification of drug-responsive cell 50 

types 8. In addition, scTenifoldKnk applies GRN perturbation to predict the functional 51 

consequences of gene knockouts 9. Collectively, these studies demonstrate that GRN-52 

informed perturbations provide a powerful approach for revealing functionally relevant 53 

cellular heterogeneity. Motivated by these studies, we reasoned that perturbing 54 

candidate TFs or genes within GRNs could provide a way to amplify subtle 55 

transcriptional differences and thereby facilitate the identification of fine-grained cell 56 

subtypes. 57 

Furthermore, different perturbations of regulatory networks can yield distinct 58 

patterns of cellular responses, making it necessary to integrate multiple perturbation 59 

outcomes to obtain stable clustering results and well-defined subpopulation 60 

boundaries. Consensus clustering provides an effective strategy to integrate multiple 61 

results and produce stable cluster assignments. Existing consensus clustering 62 

approaches typically generate ensemble diversity by repeatedly applying clustering 63 

with varying parameters or initializations to the same expression matrix 10-13 , which 64 

improves robustness to stochastic variability but fails to enhance the underlying 65 

biological signal used to distinguish cell subtypes or states. In contrast, integrating 66 

GRN-informed perturbation-derived clustering results into a consensus clustering 67 

framework captures the differential responses of subpopulations to distinct regulatory 68 

perturbations. This perturbation-aware consensus strategy leads to robust and 69 

interpretable boundaries between fine-grained cell types. 70

Building on these ideas, we develop a GRN-informed perturbation-driven 71 

consensus clustering framework named scMagnifier. By integrating with standard 72 

clustering algorithms, batch integration methods, or spatial clustering tools, 73 

scMagnifier is readily applicable to diverse scenarios—including single-batch, multi-74 

batch, and spatial transcriptomic datasets. Extensive benchmarking demonstrates that 75 

scMagnifier consistently improves the resolution and accuracy of fine-grained cell type 76 

identification and enhances the detection of rare cell populations. Additionally, 77 

scMagnifier integrates perturbation-induced clustering information into the UMAP 78 

algorithm, yielding a perturbation-aware visualization, named regulatory perturbation 79 

consensus UMAP (rpcUMAP), that provides clearer separation between cell subtypes 80 

and guides the selection of the optimal number of cell types. 81

Results 83 

Overview of scMagnifier 84 

The inputs to scMagnifier are the raw gene expression matrix (GEM) and a basic GRN, 85 

defined as a set of regulatory interactions from TFs to their target genes ( Fig. 1a ). An 86 

initial clustering is obtained using a standard scRNA-seq preprocessing and clustering 87 

pipeline implemented in Scanpy 14 . Cluster-specific GRNs are then constructed by 88 

pruning the basic GRN based on the expression levels of TFs and their target genes 89 

within each cluster ( Fig. 1d and see “Cluster-specific GRN construction” section 90 

of the Methods ). 91 

The core of scMagnifier lies in performing GRN-informed in silico perturbations 92 

and yielding an ensemble of clustering results across distinct perturbation conditions 93 

(Fig. 1b ). For each candidate TF, a cell-specific perturbation term is first defined 94 

relative to its original expression level. This perturbation is then propagated through 95 

the corresponding cluster-specific GRN to model downstream regulatory effects ( Fig. 96 

1e and see “TF genes perturbation on the level of gene expression” section of 97 

the Methods ). The resulting propagated expression changes are integrated with the 98 

original GEM to generate a post-perturbation GEM, from which a perturbation-driven 99 

clustering result is obtained. 100 

To derive stable cell subtype assignments, scMagnifier performs consensus 101 

clustering on the perturbation-driven ensemble of cluster results ( Fig. 1c, f ). To 102 

integrate these clustering results, each clustering outcome is converted into a one-hot 103 

matrix, enabling the computation of perturbation-informed cell-cell distances across 104 

the ensemble. These perturbation-informed cell-cell distances are further combined 105 

with expression-derived distances computed from the embedding matrix of the GEM, 106 

resulting in a combined distance matrix that captures both transcriptional similarity and 107 

regulatory perturbation-driven differences. Based on this combined distance matrix, a 108 

k-nearest neighbor (KNN) graph is constructed for consensus clustering and for 109 

generating a regulatory perturbation consensus UMAP (rpcUMAP) ( See “Consensus 110 

clustering” section of the Methods ). 111 

To avoid missing subtle but biologically meaningful differences, consensus 112 

clustering is initially performed at high clustering resolution. These preliminary clusters 113 

are then merged based on inter-cluster centroid distances and a minimum cluster-size 114

More generally, scMagnifier is extensible and can be applied to multi-batch single-118

scVI 17 ), enabling straightforward application to multi-batch datasets ( See “Extension 122

Benchmarking scMagnifier in real datasets 125 

To evaluate scMagnifier’s ability to resolve fine-grained cell subtypes, we 126 

benchmarked its performance on multiple publicly available single-cell datasets. For 127 

single-batch evaluation, we selected four lung adenocarcinoma datasets that contain 128 

rich cellular substructure and published annotations 18 . In each dataset, we fixed the 129 

number of output clusters to match the annotation and compared seven methods 130 

(Leiden 19 , Louvain 20 , scVI(Leiden) 17 , scVI(Louvain) 17 , SC3s 21 , scMagnifier(Leiden) and 131 

scMagnifier(Louvain)) using adjusted Rand index (ARI) and normalized mutual 132 

information (NMI) to assess agreement between clustering results and cell type 133 

annotations ( See “Benchmarking setup and parameters” section of the Methods ). 134 

Across these single-batch benchmarks, scMagnifier consistently achieved the highest 135 

ARI and NMI, irrespective of whether Leiden or Louvain was used for clustering, and 136 

outperformed a consensus clustering method (SC3s) in our comparisons ( Fig. 2a and 137 

Supplementary Table S3, S4 ). 138 

We next tested scMagnifier’s applicability in multi-batch samples. We 139 

benchmarked scMagnifier on two public multi-batch datasets (an integrated pancreas 140 

dataset 22 and the BMMC dataset 23 ). For each dataset, we compared several commonly 141 

used batch-correction methods alone and in combination with scMagnifier (BBKNN 24 ,142 

Harmony 15 , scMagnifier+Harmony, Scanorama 16 , scMagnifier+Scanorama, scVI 17 ,143 

scMagnifier+scVI). ARI and NMI were used to quantify agreement with published cell-144 

type annotations. Across both datasets, combining scMagnifier with batch-correction 145 

methods resulted in higher ARI and NMI than using batch-correction methods alone 146 

(Fig. 2b and Supplementary Table S5, S6 ). 147 

Focusing on the myeloid lineage-enriched compartment in the BMMC dataset, we 148 

found that scMagnifier+scVI precisely delineated subcluster boundaries, as validated 149 

against reference cell type annotations ( Fig. 2c ). In contrast, scVI drew an inaccurate 150 

boundary between granulocyte-monocyte progenitors (G/M prog) and CD14+ 151 

monocytes, leading to significant cross-contamination of their identities. Notably, the 152 

rpcUMAP visualization generated by scMagnifier+scVI outperformed conventional 153 

UMAP by achieving clearer separation of distinct cellular clusters. We further found 154 

that rpcUMAP identified two morphologically distinct subpopulations within Cluster 0, 155 

which were merged into a single cluster in our benchmarking analyses to align with the 156 

predefined cell-type number. Differential gene expression analysis confirmed that 157 

these subpopulations exhibited divergent expression of proliferation-associated genes 158

Finally, we explored whether scMagnifier amplifies biologically meaningful 164 

differences between cells. In the BMMC dataset, using the scMagnifier+scVI workflow, 165 

we perturbed the TF gene BCL11A and propagated its regulatory effects through 166 

cluster-specific GRNs. We observed that plasmacytoid dendritic cell (pDC) clusters 167 

exhibited progressively stronger transcriptional changes compared to neighboring cell 168

surrounding clusters ( Fig. 2e ). Importantly, BCL11A has been reported as an essential 170 

regulator of pDC development and lineage specification 25 . Its knockout leads to 171

the number and functional maturation of pDCs 25 . These observations demonstrate that 173 

gene perturbations can amplify biologically meaningful transcriptional differences, 174 

providing a mechanistic explanation for why scMagnifier can resolve fine-grained cell 175 

subtypes. 176

scMagnifier reveals hidden heterogeneity within MAIT/Th1-Th17 populations 178 

We further evaluate whether scMagnifier can reveal fine-grained cellular heterogeneity 179 

that is obscured by conventional clustering. In UPN19_pre dataset 26 , using standard 180 

Leiden clustering on the original embedding, Mucosal-associated invariant T (MAIT) 181 

cells and a T helper 1/T helper 17 (Th1/Th17)-MAIT mixed population were grouped 182 

into a single cluster (Cluster 3) and appeared continuously connected on the original 183 

UMAP, indicating limited separability in the original embedding space ( Fig. 3a, b ). Even 184 

when the initial resolution was increased, the two cell populations were still recognized 185 

as a single cluster ( Supplementary Fig. S3a ). This observation is expected, as MAIT 186 

cells exhibit transcriptional programs associated with both Th1 and Th17 effector 187 

functions 27 . In contrast, applying scMagnifier to the same cells clearly separated this 188 

previously merged population into two distinct clusters (Cluster 2 and Cluster 16) in 189 

the rpcUMAP space ( Fig. 3a, b ). 190

expression of cytotoxic-associated genes including CD8A , NKG7 , NCR3 and PRF1 ,192

scMagnifier enables the identification of rare immune cell types with distinct 220 

regulatory programs 221 

Rare cells often represent a very small fraction of the total population and are therefore 222 

susceptible to technical noise, dropout events and batch effects, while conventional 223 

clustering workflows tend to favor dominant transcriptional programs and can merge 224 

low-abundance subpopulations into larger clusters 31,32 . To test whether scMagnifier 225 

can effectively identify rare cell populations, we applied it to two independent scRNA-226 

seq datasets, EBUS_10 and LUNG_N30 18 . When exploring rare cell states, the 227 

minimum cluster-size threshold used during the cluster-merge step can be 228 

appropriately reduced based on the size of the dataset and the underlying biological 229 

context, so as to avoid merging small but potentially meaningful clusters and to retain 230 

candidate rare cell populations for downstream evaluation (See “ Consensus 231 

clustering ” section of the Methods ). 232 

In the EBUS_10 dataset, scMagnifier identified two small cell clusters, R1 (18 cells, 233 

0.40%) and R2 (16 cells, 0.36%), that could not be resolved by conventional clustering 234 

methods even at high resolution ( Supplementary Fig. S4a ). While these cells were 235 

closely positioned within larger populations in the standard UMAP embedding, they 236 

became clearly separated from surrounding clusters in the rpcUMAP space ( Fig. 4a ), 237 

suggesting the potential presence of subtle but consistent transcriptional differences 238 

that are revealed by scMagnifier. To characterize these clusters, we first grouped the 239 

remaining cells according to their original cell-type annotations. We then performed 240 

differential expression analysis for R1, R2 and each annotation-defined cluster, 241 

obtaining cluster-specific differentially expressed gene (DEG) sets for all groups. 242 

These cluster-specific DEG sets were subsequently used to quantify transcriptional 243 

similarity between R1, R2 and annotated cell populations using the Jaccard coefficient 244 

(See “Jaccard coefficient calculation” section of the Methods ). R1 showed 245 

similarity with germinal centers (GC) B cells in the dark zone (DZ) (Jaccard = 0.33) and 246

Similarly, we next applied scMagnifier to the LUNG_N30 dataset and identified a 273 

small cluster, R3 (36 cells, 1.25%), that was not detected by conventional high-274

often linked to early stages of activation and immune responses 41 .290

Integration of scMagnifier with STAGATE reveals tumor cell subtypes and spatial 296 

organization in ovarian cancer 297 

The identification of tumor cell subtypes and their spatial organization plays a critical 298 

role in understanding cancer progression and therapeutic response 42 . STAGATE 299 

enables the accurate identification of spatial domains by learning low-dimensional 300 

latent embeddings 43 , while scMagnifier provides fine-grained types of cells. Hence, we 301 

integrated scMagnifier with STAGATE (See “Integration of scMagnifier with 302 

STAGATE for Spatial Transcriptomics Analysis” section of the Methods ), aiming 303 

to identify biologically meaningful tumor subtypes and illustrate their spatial domains. 304 

We used the spatial transcriptomics dataset of epithelial ovarian cancer from the 305 

SPATCH website 44 , which incorporates both detailed cell annotations and spatial layer 306 

annotations. For our analysis, we selected a subset of cells located at the center of 307 

spatial coordinates comprising 48,793 cells ( Fig. 5b ). 308 

Epithelial ovarian cancer, a malignancy originating from epithelial cells, is 309 

characterized by significant cellular heterogeneity and complex tumor 310 

microenvironment 45 . Based on cell annotations and spatial layer annotations provided 311 

by the dataset ( Fig. 5a ), we determined the approximate location of the tumor’s core 312 

regions (The overlapping region of Epithelial cell clusters in the cell type annotation 313 

map and Layer 3 regions in the spatial layer annotation map). Subsequently, using the 314 

final clustering results from scMagnifier combined with STAGATE, we identified five 315 

distinct subclusters of tumor cells (Cluster 0, Cluster 2, Cluster 4, Cluster 7 and Cluster 316 

9), which correspond to potential tumor core regions ( Fig. 5a, b ). Using differential 317 

gene expression analysis and functional enrichment analysis, we confirmed that the 318 

five tumor subclusters exhibit distinct molecular and functional differences ( Fig. 5c, d ). 319 

Notably, we observed that the spatial localization of Cluster 2 closely overlapped 320 

with the deeply stained regions in H&E histology ( Fig. 5e ). Differential gene expression 321 

analysis revealed that Cluster 2 displayed high expression of IGF2 (Fig. 5c ). It has 322 

been reported IGF2 is involved in tumor growth and invasion mechanisms 46 .323 

Consistent with this, functional enrichment analysis identified pathways related to 324 

negative regulation of apoptosis (Adjusted p-value=2.0×10 -7 ) and extracellular 325 

structure organization (Adjusted p-value=3.9×10 -8 ) in Cluster 2 ( Fig. 5d ), indicating 326 

that these cells may actively evade cell death and promote metastatic potential. This 327 

functional profile aligns well with IGF2 -mediated proliferative and anti -apoptotic 328 

signaling, a characteristic feature of aggressive epithelial tumor subpopulations 46 .329 

Importantly, deep staining in H&E images generally indicates high cellular density and 330 

malignancy, which are hallmarks of invasive tumor subpopulations 47 , and further 331 

supports that Cluster 2 corresponds to a highly aggressive tumor subpopulation. 332 

Collectively, this spatial correlation suggests that, by integrating scMagnifier with 333 

STAGATE, we were able to identify high-invasion tumor regions without relying on 334

Discussion 349 

Accurately delineating cell subpopulations and their boundaries remains challenging, 350 

particularly when transcriptional differences are subtle or obscured by noise, batch 351 

effects. In this study, we presented scMagnifier, a regulatory perturbation-driven 352 

consensus clustering framework designed to resolve fine-grained cell subtypes. 353 

scMagnifier first amplifies subtle regulatory differences through systematic 354 

perturbation of TF genes to reveal latent subclusters, and then integrates clustering 355 

outcomes across multiple perturbations via consensus clustering to obtain stable 356 

subtype assignments and well-defined boundaries. We demonstrated practical utility 357 

of scMagnifier in several applications. We found scMagnifier clearly reveals hidden 358 

heterogeneity within MAIT/Th1-Th17 populations. We additionally substantiated the 359 

ability of scMagnifier to detect rare cell populations. Finally, by integrating scMagnifier 360 

and STAGATE, we identified five subtypes of ovarian cancer and detected invasive 361 

regions without incorporating pathological images. 362 

Recent studies have demonstrated that GRN perturbation models are capable of 363 

uncovering latent biological signals 7-9 . Building on this framework, we showed that 364 

perturbing TF genes at the gene expression level amplifies the underlying biological 365 

differences, thereby enabling the identification of subclusters ( Fig. 2e and Fig. 5f ). It 366 

is precisely this perturbation strategy that supports the key advantage of scMagnifier. 367 

However, our perturbations do not explicitly model true post-perturbation cellular states, 368 

as they rely on GRN-based signal propagation at the transcriptomic level rather than 369 

learning distributional shifts induced by real perturbations. To address this limitation 370 

and enhance biological fidelity, future extensions could integrate scMagnifier with 371 

optimal transport-based perturbation models such as CellOT 48 , which learn mappings 372 

between control and perturbed single-cell distributions, thereby improving the 373 

biological realism of perturbation effects while retaining scMagnifier’s ability to amplify 374 

regulatory differences for fine-grained subcluster discovery. 375 

scMagnifier effectively identifies heterogeneous cell populations, which form 376 

clearly separated clusters from the remaining cells in the rpcUMAP embedding ( Fig 3a 377 

and Fig. 4a, d ). This clear separation stems from two synergistic factors: first, the 378

Methods 390 

Data preprocessing 391 

All datasets were prepared in the AnnData format and processed using Scanpy 14 . First, 392 

lowly expressed genes were filtered out by retaining only genes with at least one count 393 

across all cells. Expression values were then normalized per cell to a total UMI count 394 

of 10,000. Highly variable genes (HVGs) were identified on the normalized non-log-395 

transformed expression matrix, and the top 2,000 HVGs were retained. After 396 

subsetting the dataset to these HVGs, expression values were renormalized per cell 397 

to a total UMI count of 10,000 to account for the effects of gene filtering. Importantly, 398 

the renormalized non-log-transformed GEM was retained and used for downstream 399

Cluster-specific GRN construction 406 

Cluster-specific GRNs were constructed following the standard CellOracle framework 7.407 

As prior regulatory information, we employed the human promoter-based GRN 408 

provided by CellOracle as our basic GRN, which defines potential TF gene-target gene 409 

relationships. Cluster-specific GRNs were constructed based on the basic GRN and 410 

initial clustering results, using the renormalized but non-log-transformed GEM stored 411 

during data preprocessing. Firstly, dimensionality reduction was performed using PCA 412 

(oracle.perform_PCA() function), followed by KNN-based imputation 413 

(oracle.knn_imputation() function) to alleviate data sparsity. Regulatory relationships 414 

between TF genes and their putative target genes were modeled using regression-415 

based approaches (oracle.get_links() function), and cluster-specific regulatory links 416 

were further identified and filtered based on statistical significance and network scores 417 

(links.filter_links() function). Finally, quantitative GRN models for perturbation 418 

simulation were fitted (oracle.fit_GRN_for_simulation() function). All parameters were 419 

set according to the default or recommended settings in CellOracle. 420

Extension of scMagnifier for multi-batch datasets 525 

scMagnifier can be adapted to handle multi-batch datasets by integrating it with batch 526 

effect correction methods that operate in the dimensionality reduction space, such as 527 

Harmony 15 , Scanorama 16 or scVI 17 . Specifically, during the algorithm workflow, we 528 

make two key adjustments. First, during data preprocessing and TF genes perturbation, 529 

we replace PCA-based dimensionality reduction with a batch effect correction method. 530 

Second, in the consensus clustering step, we use the embedding obtained from the 531 

batch effect correction method to compute cell distances in the reduced space, instead 532 

of using the PCA matrix. All other steps remain unchanged. 533

Integration of scMagnifier with STAGATE for Spatial Transcriptomics Analysis 608 

To integrate scMagnifier with STAGATE 43 for spatial transcriptomics analysis, we 609 

followed the same extension strategy as described for applying scMagnifier to multi-610 

batch datasets. Specifically, STAGATE was used to generate a low-dimensional 611 

embedding that captures spatial context, and this embedding replaced the PCA space 612 

in the scMagnifier workflow. During data preprocessing and TF gene perturbation, the 613 

STAGATE-derived latent embedding was used for downstream analyses instead of 614 

PCA. In the consensus clustering step, cell-cell distances in the reduced space were 615 

computed based on the STAGATE embedding, while all other steps of the scMagnifier 616 

algorithm remained unchanged. 617 

In our dataset, Spatial neighbor graphs in STAGATE were constructed using a 618 

KNN-based strategy ( k=10). STAGATE was trained for 300 epochs to obtain stable 619 

spatial embeddings. Downstream clustering was performed using the Leiden algorithm 620 

with a resolution of 0.3. 621
