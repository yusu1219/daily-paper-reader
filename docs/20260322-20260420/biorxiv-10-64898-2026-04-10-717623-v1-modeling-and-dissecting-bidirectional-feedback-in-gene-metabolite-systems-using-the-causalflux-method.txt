# Abstract 

Predicting cellular behaviors, a central task in systems biology and metabolic engineering, can be enhanced through integrative modeling of processes such as gene regulation and metabolism. Information flow from gene regulation (modeled via a gene regulatory network) to metabolism (modeled via a genome-scale metabolic model) is well-studied, but the reciprocal regulation of genes by metabolites is less explored. We introduce CausalFlux, a method that models bidirectional feedback between genes and metabolites, in order to predict steady-state reaction fluxes under wild-type (WT) or perturbed (e.g., gene knockout/KO) conditions. CausalFlux does so by iteratively performing causal surgery on a Bayesian gene regulatory network and constraint-based analysis of a coupled metabolic model. CausalFlux enabled us to assess the impact of two-way feedback in several testbed models and real-world biological systems by comparing its predictions to those of TRIMER, a state-of-the-art model of gene-to-metabolite one-way feedback. Incorporating bidirectional feedback, as in CausalFlux, improved the Spearman correlation between actual and predicted fluxes in 92% of the 39 distinct simulation conditions relative to TRIMER. For predicting growth/no-growth phenotype following single-gene KOs in E. coli , CausalFlux achieved a balanced accuracy of 0 .79 in identifying essential genes, and TRIMER achieved 0 .71 for the same task, again highlighting the importance of modeling two-way feedback. In ablation studies that further dissect the role of specific metabolite →gene feedback edges in E. coli , the F1 scores of gene essentiality predictions decreased by 7 .5% and 13% upon ablation of feedback edges from any metabolite to the crp gene and the 10 metabolic feedback genes with the highest influence on the KO genes, respectively. Finally, we highlight the application of CausalFlux to predict the essentiality of several hundred genes under different media conditions. # Author summary 

The myriad processes within a living cell, such as gene regulation or metabolism, are tightly interconnected. Modeling these interconnected processes can offer a deeper mechanistic understanding of cellular behaviors, as well as guide efforts that engineer the metabolic output of a cell. In this work, we develop a novel integrated model of gene regulation and metabolism that incorporates bidirectional feedback between these two processes, via the concept of metabolite-induced causal surgery on a gene regulatory network and gene-induced constraints on the fluxes of metabolic reactions. Our model, which we call CausalFlux, represents an advance over most existing models that capture just the one-way gene-to-metabolism feedback (i.e., genes coding for enzymes that control metabolic reactions). Our CausalFlux methodology opens up an unique opportunity to quantify the impact of two-way feedback in gene-metabolite systems, via comparison of CausalFlux’s predictions to those of TRIMER, a published model incorporating one-way feedback alone. For predicting reaction fluxes in testbed models and essential genes in E. coli , quantitative comparison of the performance of CausalFlux vs. TRIMER showed that accounting for two-way feedback leads to more accurate and biologically meaningful predictions. CausalFlux also enabled us to quantify the effect of two-way feedback by comparing prediction performance before and after ablation of certain feedback edges from metabolites to genes. Overall, our findings highlight the importance of modeling gene regulation and metabolism as two-way interconnected systems within a living cell, and encourage future works to incorporate gene ↔metabolite feedback into their analyses. 

# Introduction 1

Thousands of genome-scale metabolic models (GSMM) have been constructed in the 2

past two decades. These network-based models comprehensively represent an organism’s 3

metabolism, capturing the network of biochemical reactions as a stoichiometric matrix 4

and gene-protein-reaction (GPR) rules that govern the activity of specific metabolic 5

reaction. Very often, the gene regulatory state of the cell inferred via transcriptomic 6

(gene expression) measurements and/or knowledge of the gene regulatory network 7

(GRN) is integrated as a constraint to improve GSMMs’ predictions. Existing methods 8

that integrate gene regulatory constraints into GSMMs can be broadly classified into 9

three categories [ 1 ]: (i) Integration through transcriptomics data: These methods filter 10 

out (or capture) certain metabolic reactions by evaluating the GPR rules using the gene 11 

expression evidence, thereby representing the metabolic reactions as either ON (active) 12 

or OFF (inactive). Examples include context-specific model-building algorithms such as 13 

GIMME [ 2], and Fastcore [ 3]. One of the major limitations of these approaches is their 14 

inability to predict fluxes when gene expression data corresponding to the KO 15 

(Knockout) perturbation of interest is not available; (ii) Integration through Boolean 16 

GRNs: Methods like rFBA [ 4 , 5] and SR-FBA [ 6, 7 ] represent gene expression states as 17 

ON/OFF and model regulatory programs using a network of Boolean relations involving 18 

these genes. They also model metabolic regulation of genes into this Boolean network 19 

formulation to capture bidirectional gene-metabolite feedback, but they require 20 

extensive literature searches to define the Boolean relations/rules and can be 21

Multi-omics studies on E. coli and yeast have revealed the presence of certain 29 

metabolites that regulate the expression of genes in the GRN [10], thereby exerting 30 

feedback on gene expression and creating crosstalk between metabolic and regulatory 31 

systems. Carthew provides examples of metabolites acting as signaling molecules to 32 

regulate genes in the GRN [11]. Kumar et al. utilize this knowledge of metabolite 33 

feedback to construct different network modules of the GRN, both with and without 34 

feedback, to understand the underlying causal structure of the GRN in E. coli and 35 

B. subtilis [12]. These studies emphasize that incorporating metabolic feedback into the 36 

GRN is crucial to obtain a tightly integrated model of the cellular subsystems involved, 37 

and thereby encourages extending current efforts that mostly model one-way feedback 38 

from gene regulation to metabolism to explicitly capture bidirectional feedback. 39 

Towards addressing the above research gap, this study makes the following broad 40 

contributions: 41 

i Methodological: We propose ‘CausalFlux’, an integrated framework that captures 42 

the complex interactions within and between the metabolic and gene networks 43 

(see Fig. 1[A–C]). CausalFlux specifically models the bidirectional feedback 44 

between gene regulation and metabolism in an iterative fashion. CausalFlux’s 45 

unique contribution is to model metabolite-to-gene interactions, i.e., feedback 46 

metabolites that regulate gene activity, via the techniques of iterative causal 47 

surgery on a Bayesian network representation of GRN, and constraint-based 48 

analysis of a coupled metabolic model (see Fig. 1[B–C]). 49 

ii Conceptual: Equipped with CausalFlux, we systematically quantified the impact 50 

of bidirectional feedback in a cellular system by comparing CausalFlux’s 51 

predictive performance to that of TRIMER, and via ablation of certain feedback 52 

edges. We performed the comparative analyses on testbed models (TMs) with 53 

simulated steady-state fluxes, and a real-world E. coli dataset with ground-truth 54 

information on growth/no-growth phenotype of 798 single-gene knockouts (KOs; 55 

see Fig. 1[D–E]); and ablation studies on the E. coli dataset. 56 

iii Application: We highlight an application of CausalFlux to predict gene essentiality 57 

of several hundred genes under different growth media conditions (minimal to rich 58 

media). Such prospective predictions can aid metabolic engineering efforts by 59 

prioritizing single-gene KOs that yield the desired phenotype of interest. 60 

Overall, the results from the analyses and application above showed that the 61 

CausalFlux method that we developed can crucially utilize information on feedback 62 

metabolites to predict trends in reaction fluxes and qualitative (growth/no-growth) 63 

outcomes; thereby encouraging future systems modeling efforts to carefully incorporate 64 

not only gene-to-metabolite but also metabolite-to-gene interactions. 65

Effect of feedback-imparting iterations on the CausalFlux outcome: To better 131 

understand the role of feedback in our method, we examined whether the actual fluxes 132 

were more strongly correlated with the fluxes predicted from successive iterations of 133 

CausalFlux (compared to its earlier iterations where feedback is incorporated to a lesser 134 

extent). ρ was computed between predictions from iteration 1 and the actual fluxes 135 

across all 39 TM cases, and similarly between predictions from iteration 2 and the 136 

actual fluxes. We observed that in certain cases, such as the “WT” and “X” knockouts 137 

in TM1 and TM2, respectively, feedback improved performance, as reflected by 138 

increased ρ values in successive iterations (see S4 Fig). In contrast, for most of the 139 

other cases, the data points lie close to the x = y line, indicating that feedback had 140 

little effect. In a few specific scenarios, such as the “I” knockout in TM2 and the 3200 141 

exchange rate condition in TM1, feedback appeared to have a negative impact. 142 

Together, all of the above observations suggest that the influence of feedback is 143 

context-dependent, and a more careful examination in real-world settings is required to 144 

understand its effects fully, which we perform next. 145 

Real-world evaluation: Using CausalFlux to predict 146 

growth/no-growth phenotype in E. coli 147 

We applied CausalFlux and TRIMER to computationally model 798 single-gene KOs in 148 

E. coli (see Methods on implementation) to predict fluxes. For each gene KO, if the 149 

predicted flux for the biomass reaction is 0, then a “no-growth” phenotype is predicted 150 

(i.e., the gene is predicted as an essential gene). If the gene KO leads to a prediction of 151 

non-zero biomass flux, then we predict a growth phenotype (i.e., that the gene is 152 

non-essential). Of all single-gene knockouts considered, 47 genes showed zero growth 153 

upon knockout in the Keio collection and were therefore treated as ground-truth 154 

essential; the rest were called ground-truth non-essential genes [13]. 155 

CausalFlux performs better in predicting the no-growth cases (essential genes) 156 

compared to TRIMER, and a random classifier, as seen in Fig. 4A. Here, the random 157 

classifier refers to a classifier that predicts all the instances as the positive class 158 

(essential or no-growth cases). CausalFlux predictions were better than those of 159 

TRIMER in terms of F1 score and balanced accuracy (see Fig. 4A). Of the 47 160 

ground-truth essential genes, 29 were accurately predicted by CausalFlux. While 161 

TRIMER accurately predicted 22 out of the 47 essential genes, it also predicted a larger 162 

number of false positives. 163 

To assess the impact of metabolic feedback on CausalFlux predictions, we compared 164 

the results and corresponding metrics at the end of three iterations of the algorithm: 165 

the 0 th iteration (without any metabolic feedback), the 1 st iteration (after one round of 166 

feedback incorporation), and the final iteration (the final converged state after multiple 167 

feedback iterations), as shown in Fig. 4[B–C]. An improvement in both precision and F1 168 

score can be clearly observed across these iterations, indicating that incorporating 169 

metabolic feedback enhances the performance of CausalFlux. We could also observe 170 

changes in true negatives (from 751 → 709 → 717) and false positives (from 171 

0 → 42 → 34) over iterations. These findings show that feedback-imparting iterations 172 

are useful to enhance the performance of CausalFlux in real-world E. coli benchmarks. 173

# Discussion 304 

Integrating gene regulatory networks (GRNs) and genome-scale metabolic models 305 

(GSMMs) while accounting for bidirectional feedback mechanisms is crucial for 306 

accurately modeling and understanding the complex interplay within biological systems. 307 

In this work, we introduce CausalFlux, a novel methodology that integrates GRNs and 308 

GSMMs to predict reaction fluxes under gene KO perturbations. CausalFlux operates 309 

through an iterative process that combines causal surgery on the GRN to simulate the 310 

impact of gene KOs with constraint-based analysis of the GSMM to predict the 311 

resulting metabolic fluxes. We evaluated the performance of CausalFlux using three 312 

testbed datasets, which enabled direct comparison of predicted steady-state fluxes with 313 

ground-truth values obtained from ODE-based simulations. Across most evaluation 314 

scenarios, CausalFlux demonstrated a clear advantage over TRIMER in capturing 315 

trends in biomass fluxes across different testbed models, and this performance 316 

advantage was consistently observed under varying conditions, such as different 317 

exchange rates. These results underscore the importance of incorporating feedback 318 

mechanisms into integrated modeling frameworks. We further applied CausalFlux to 319 

predict growth versus no-growth phenotypes in E. coli across 798 single-gene KO cases, 320 

achieving a balanced accuracy of 0.79, which again exceeded that of TRIMER. 321 

Additionally, we predicted gene essentiality for these 798 genes under multiple growth 322 

media conditions, including TSB and M9 minimal media. Together, these analyses 323 

demonstrate the potential applicability of our algorithm. Overall, our results suggest 324 

that CausalFlux effectively captures reaction flux trends (particularly qualitative trends 325 

such as significant increases or decreases in reaction fluxes or the ability to distinguish 326 

zero versus nonzero growth outcomes) under gene KO perturbations. 327 

In testbed models, we clearly see that Causalflux outperforms other state-of-the-art 328 

method like TRIMER in most of the KO conditions across all the testbed models. 329 

While the existing method GIMME exhibited comparable to or sometimes better 330 

performance than CausalFlux in predicting biomass reaction fluxes of testbed models, it 331

qualitative predictions of reaction fluxes. This limitation arises from the lack of kinetic 359 

parameters, which are essential for precise quantitative modeling of the integrated 360 

system dynamics. Also, our algorithm can predict only single-gene KOs and at present 361 

cannot handle multiple-gene KOs. In addition to comparing our method with TRIMER, 362 

we also evaluated our approach with earlier approaches like rFBA and PROM [5, 8]. 363 

Since rFBA is significantly older and no functional implementation is currently 364 

available, a direct evaluation on predicting the essentiality of the 798 genes in E. coli 365 

was not feasible. However, PROM benchmarks itself with rFBA and provides the 366 

results for both methods on a separate dataset and gene KO conditions. We therefore 367 

assessed our method on the same dataset and gene KO cases as performed by 368 

Chandrasekaran et al., and compared our findings with the published performance 369 

values for rFBA and PROM to allow a fair comparison. Our findings demonstrate that 370 

CausalFlux outperforms rFBA at all thresholds used to classify gene KOs as essential 371 

(no-growth cases) or non-essential (growth cases) on the dataset utilized by 372 

Chandrasekaran et al. Relative to PROM, our method has comparable performance in 373 

one of the three thresholds, while PROM remains better in others. These comparisons 374 

show that CausalFlux remains competitive with both recent and earlier methods. 375 

Nevertheless, CausalFlux’s ability to capture bidirectional interactions between the 376 

gene regulatory and metabolic networks offers a more complete understanding of 377 

cellular behaviour and has the potential to significantly impact fields such as metabolic 378 

engineering, drug discovery, and systems biology. In future, this methodology can be 379 

potentially applied to other model organisms, such as yeast and B. subtilis to 380 

understand the bidirectional feedback. 381

# Materials and Methods 382 

Background on separate GSMM and GRN models 383 

GSMM models a network of interconnected metabolic reactions in a cellular system 384 

using six components: set of all metabolic reactions in the model ( n reactions), set of all 385 

metabolites participating in these reactions ( m metabolites), stoichiometric matrix 386 

(S ∈ Rm×n) representing the metabolite substrates and products of the reactions, 387 

vector of lower bounds on reaction fluxes ( vlb ∈ Rn), vector of upper bounds on 388 

reaction fluxes ( vub ∈ Rn), and set of gene-protein-reaction ( GP R ) relations. A GPR 389 

relation specifies the activity of a metabolic reaction using Boolean-logic-based rules 390 

(specifically, a Boolean expression composed of “AND/OR” operators acting on the 391 

expression states of genes, such as enzyme-coding genes, that regulate the reaction). 392 

Steady-state fluxes of reactions in a GSMM can be predicted using well-known methods 393 

such as Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) [23] and Flux Variability Analysis (FVA) [24]. 394 

On the other hand, GRN model of transcriptional regulation is a directed 395 

probabilistic graphical model, also known as a Bayesian network [ 25 ]. This Bayesian or 396 

probabilistic GRN model has two components: (i) [GRN structure] a directed graph 397 

whose nodes are all genes in the system (including transcription factors and target 398 

genes), and directed edges represent regulatory influences between them, such as the 399 

control of a target gene by a transcription factor; and (ii) [probabilistic model] a set of 400 

parameters that describe the probability distributions governing the binary expression 401 

states of all genes in the network (with each node/gene associated with a binary-valued 402 

random variable that is 0 for inactive and 1 for active state of the gene). Being a 403 

Bayesian network, the graph is directed and acyclic, and the joint distribution of its 404 

random variables factorizes as the product of the conditional distribution of each gene 405 

given its parents in the GRN. That is, P r [all genes in GRN] = 406

CausalFlux method 412 

CausalFlux integrates the separate models of the two cellular subsystems described 413 

above (GSMM for metabolism and GRN for transcriptional/gene regulation) by 414 

crucially utilizing the bidirectional feedback interactions between them. The goal is to 415 

simulate the coupled GRN and GSMM models, and thereby predict the steady-state flux 416 

of reactions in the GSMM under this two-way feedback. The GRN-to-GSMM feedback 417 

is specified using the GPR rules, and the reciprocal GSMM-to-GRN feedback is specified 418 

by a set of feedback metabolites ( Mf b ⊆ all metabolites in GSMM) and the genes they 419 

regulate. A key contribution of CausalFlux is to realize this reciprocal feedback using 420 

the concept of “causal surgery” on the Bayesian GRN and of “sink reactions” added to 421 

the GSMM, both of which are elaborated in this section as part of the overall workflow. 422 

Given GRN, GSMM, and feedback information (GPR rules and genes regulated by 423 

Mf b ) as the primary inputs, our overall CausalFlux method consists of a one-time 424 

initialization procedure, followed by an integration step that is executed iteratively until 425 

convergence (see Fig. 1C and the pseudocode in Algorithm 1 below). To complete the 426

CausalFlux: Intialization procedure 430 

GRN Initialization (Bayesian network learning and initial surgery): Given a 431 

genome-wide gene expression (transcriptomic) dataset and a prior GRN structure as 432 

input, we learn the Bayesian network structure and associated parameters of the 433 

Bayesian GRN using the R package bnlearn [26]. In detail, we used the hill-climbing 434 

heuristic in bnlearn for Bayesian network structure learning from the binarized gene 435 

expression dataset and the prior edges present between the genes (provided as a “white 436 

list” of edges to be required to be present in the learnt structure). Binarization of gene 437 

expression data is done in a dataset-specific fashion, and details on the same are given 438 

in the Methods sections below on application of CausalFlux to different 439 

datasets/models. For structure learning, Bayesian Dirichlet equivalent was used as the 440 

score/objective function, and other hyperparameters were set at default values, except 441 

for the maximum number of parents and maximum iterations, which were set at 5 and 442 

100, respectively. The learnt structure will contain all the prior edges (“white list”) and 443 

possibly also additional edges supported by the data. With this learnt structure, we 444 

perform a final maximum likelihood-based parameter estimation to learn the conditional 445 

probability distribution of each gene in the GRN. The procedure explained here 446 

corresponds to LearnBayesianNetworkStructureAndParameter step in Algorithm 1. 447

causal perturbation. This initial perturbation is realized via causal surgery of the 449

CausalFlux: Integration step 478 

Causal surgery to mutilate GRN and perform inference: Causal surgery on a 479 

random variable in a (causal) Bayesian network is an ideal intervention on the variable, 480 

in which its value alone is externally fixed and its dependence on its parent variables is 481 

removed [ 25 ]. More generally, any intervention on a node in a causal graph is referred to 482 

as causal surgery [ 27]. Such an intervention severs the incoming edges from the parents 483 

of the target node, resulting in a mutilated graph, and fixes the conditional probability 484 

of the intervened node to a specified value. Mathematically, this operation is represented 485 

using the “do-operator”, which defines the probability distribution of a target variable 486 

when a conditioning variable is externally set to a particular value. A key contribution 487 

of the CausalFlux framework is its ability to simulate feedback effects of metabolites on 488 

genes through causal surgery on appropriate genes. This procedure is formalized in the 489 

PerformCausalSurgery step of Algorithm 1, which takes as input the GRN along with 490 

sets of repressed and active genes. Using the “do-operator”, genes in the repressed set 491 

are fixed to 0, while genes in the active set are fixed to 1, after removing the 492 

corresponding parental edges in the GRN. Importantly, causal surgeries on multiple 493 

genes can be performed in any order without affecting the resulting GRN structure. 494 

To illustrate this process, consider Fig. 1B. Prior to causal surgery, the conditional 495 

probability of gene “D” depends on gene “C”, expressed as P (D = 1 | C = 1), where 496 

the state of “C” itself depends on its parent genes “A” and “B”. When a metabolite 497 

(M2) activates gene “C”, the “do-operator” is applied to set C = 1, yielding 498 

P (D = 1 | do (C = 1)). This intervention removes the incoming edges from “A” and “B” 499 

to “C” and fixes the activity of “C” to 1. 500

Testbed Model (TM) benchmarks 556 

To evaluate CausalFlux’s predictions, we consider three simplified TMs of integrated 557 

GRN-GSMM systems (labeled TM1, TM2, and TM3). A testbed model is specified 558 

using its topology (network structure), and a system of ODEs based on this topology 559 

that govern the dynamics of the coupled GRN-GSMM system. Simulating these ODEs 560 

yields ground-truth steady-state fluxes of reactions in the testbed model, which can 561 

then be compared to steady-state fluxes predicted by CausalFlux applied on the testbed 562 

model. 563 

Topology of TMs 564 

A testbed model consists of a small-scale GRN-like and GSMM-like component. The 565 

network motifs prominent in biological systems served as the basis for constructing the 566 

GRN components in the testbed models. We incorporated some well-known network 567 

motifs, such as feedforward and feedback loops, single-input modules, and dense 568 

overlapping regulons, into our testbed models due to their prevalence in the 569 

transcription networks of biological systems [29]. The small-scale GSMM comprises a 570 

few reactions, which operate on metabolites, and are catalyzed by enzymes (as per the 571 

GPR rules based on enzyme-encoding genes). For simplicity, we formulate all these 572 

metabolic reactions as irreversible reactions. In all TMs, we treat reaction R1 as the 573 

exchange (nutrient import) reaction. We specify the biomass reaction to be the reaction 574 

R4 in TM1, R11 in TM2, and R8 in TM3. This specification is required when three 575 

methods, CausalFlux, TRIMER, and GIMME, are applied on the testbed models to 576 

predict fluxes. The topology of TM1 is depicted in Fig. 2A (and of TM2 and TM3 in 577 

S1 Fig), and the components of TMs (including metabolic reactions and gene-gene 578 

interactions) are in Table S1. 579

Applying CausalFlux following ablation of metabolite-to-gene 775 

feedback edges 776 

The metabolite-to-gene feedback interactions in E. coli considered in this work involved 777 

54 unique genes, referred to as metabolic feedback genes. We define the influence of any 778 

such metabolic feedback gene (on the single-gene KOs) as the number of single-KO 779 

genes that are in the 0-hop or 1-hop neighbourhood of that gene in the GRN. The 780 

metabolic feedback genes can then be ranked based on this influence measure. We 781 

conducted five separate ablation studies in which the feedback edges from metabolite(s) 782 

to gene(s) were removed and the change in prediction performance noted. Specifically, 783 

in the first three studies, we ablated feedback edges to crp , fur , and cra , which are the 784 

metabolic feedback genes with the highest, second-highest, and third-highest influence 785 

(on the single-gene KOs) respectively. In the final two ablation studies, we considered a 786 

set of 10 (and separately 40) metabolic feedback genes with the most influence on the 787 

single-KO genes (with the latter representing approximately 75% of the total 54 genes) 788 

and removed the incoming feedback edges from metabolites to all genes in the set. After 789 

performing any of the above ablations of metabolite-to-gene edges, the CausalFlux 790 

algorithm was applied to predict the biomass for each of the 798 single-gene KO 791 

conditions, and these predictions compared to the ones obtained before ablation. 792
