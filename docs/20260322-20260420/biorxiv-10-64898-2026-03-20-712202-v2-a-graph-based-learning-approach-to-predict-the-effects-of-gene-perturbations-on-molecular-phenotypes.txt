# Abstract 

Motivation: Large-scale gene knockdown/knockout screens have been used to gain insight into a wide array of phenotypes and biological processes. However, conducting such experiments is expensive and labor-intensive. In this work, we present a general graph-based machine-learning approach that can predict the effects of gene perturbations on molecular phenotypes of interest given some measured phenotypic effects of other gene perturbations. The motivation for learning models that can predict the effects of gene perturbations is fourfold. Such models can (1) predict effects for unmeasured genes in cases in which cost or technical barriers preclude perturbing every gene, (2) prioritize unmeasured genes or sets of genes for subsequent perturbation experiments, (3) hypothesize mechanisms that underlie the relationships between the perturbed genes and their effects, and (4) generalize to other unmeasured phenotypes of interest. 

Results: We evaluate our approach by applying it, in conjunction with four different learning methods, to learn models for four varied phenotypes. Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that the learned models (1) show relatively high levels of predictive accuracy across the four phenotypes, (2) have better predictive accuracy than several standard baselines, (3) can often learn accurate models with small training sets, (4) benefit from having multiple sources of evidence in the input representation, (5) can, in many cases, transfer their predictive value to other phenotypes. 

Data availablity: The assembled data sets and source code for this work are available at: https://github.com/Craven-Biostat-Lab/graph-molecular-phenotype-prediction

# Introduction 1

Genetic perturbation methods can reveal causal relationships between genetic elements 2

and molecular or cellular phenotypes of interest. More specifically, methods for either 3

knocking out or knocking down genes, including CRISPR gene editing and RNA 4

interference, can identify the genes and pathways involved in such phenotypes. 5

High-throughput gene-perturbation screens have been used to uncover the genes 6

involved in a broad range of phenotypes including cell morphology (1), cholesterol 7

metabolism (2), host-pathogen interactions (3), and mitochondrial metabolism (4) 8

among many others. 9

Here, we present a general, graph-based machine learning approach developed to 10 

predict the effects of gene perturbations on molecular phenotypes of interest. The 11 

motivation for learning models that can predict the effects of gene perturbations is 12 

fourfold. Such models can (1) predict effects for unmeasured genes in cases in which 13 

cost or technical barriers preclude perturbing every gene, (2) prioritize unmeasured 14 

genes or sets of genes for subsequent perturbation experiments, (3) hypothesize 15 

mechanisms that underlie the relationships between the perturbed genes and their 16 

effects, and (4) generalize to other unmeasured phenotypes of interest. 17 

Several methods have been developed to predict scalar phenotypic changes from gene 18 

perturbations. Among them, DeepEP (5), DeepHE (6), and EPGAT (7) are 19 

knowledge-graph-based, deep learning approaches designed to predict which human 20 

genes are essential. DeepEP and DeepHE extract low-dimensional representations of 21 

genes from a protein-protein interaction (PPI) network using node2vec (8). DeepEP 22 

integrates these representations with gene expression profiles whereas DeepHE combines 23 

them with DNA-sequence features to predict essential genes. EPGAT leverages graph 24 

attention networks to predict essential genes using a PPI network, and orthology and 25 

subcellular localization features. A notable limitation of these methods is that they are 26 

restricted to predicting a single scalar phenotype (cell survival, in the applications 27 

considered). Moreover, each learned model is able to make predictions about only one 28 

phenotype. 29 

Another class of methods have been developed to predict the effects of genetic 30 

perturbations on gene expression. Given pre-perturbation single-cell gene expression 31 

profiles and a set of perturbed genes, these models predict the resulting transcriptomic 32 

responses. GEARS (9) incorporates a Gene Ontology (GO) graph and a gene 33 

coexpression graph with deep neural networks to predict the effects of perturbations. 34 

Similarly, BioDSNN (10) employs a variational autoencoder to generate 35 

post-perturbation gene expression profiles, utilizing the same graphs as GEARS with 36 

the addition of Reactome pathways (11). A limitation of these approaches is that they 37 

focus solely on predicting changes to gene expression profiles and do not generalize to 38 

other phenotypes. 39 

In contrast to all these previously developed methods, our approach generalizes to a 40 

wide range of molecular and cellular phenotypes, and can learn a single model to predict 41 

multiple phenotypes. 42

The knowledge graph employed in our approach represents biomolecular entities and 68

the Gene Ontology (15). 83 

The learning task we consider is to infer a model f (·) that takes a feature vector 84

n(g) representing attributes of source node g, a feature vector e(g→P ) characterizing the 88 

relationship between g and P and the paths that connect them, and a feature vector 89 

n(P ), representing attributes of the node(s) in P to which g is connected by a path. 90 

In this setting, a model can be trained on a set of gene-phenotype pairs and then 91 

potentially be used to make predictions for genes not in the training set, phenotypes not 92 

in the training set, or both. The models can be learned using a broad range of learning 93 

algorithms that assume feature vector input representations. 94 

## Feature Representation 95 

As noted above, the feature vector x(g,P ) is constructed by concatenating a vector n(g) 96 

representing node g, a vector n(P ), representing the nodes in P to which g is connected 97 

by paths, and a vector e(g→P ) characterizing the relationship between g and P and the 98 

paths that connect them. We refer to these three constituent vectors as the source 99 

features , target features , and the source-target relation features , respectively. The 100 

features are computed using the diverse types of information represented in the 101 

knowledge graph. 102 

The source features n(g) and target features n(P ) are computed from the same types 103 

of information and have the same dimensionality. There are three types of source and 104 

target features. The first, which we refer to as the cellular abundance features, represent 105 

each gene product’s abundance in the relevant cell line, and when available, cell type. 106 

RNA abundances for the cell line and cell type and protein abundance for the cell type 107 

are all extracted from the Human Protein Atlas. 108

The second set of source and target features are the subcellular localization features. 109 

We use 41 binary features to represent a hierarchical taxonomy of subcellular 110 

compartments. These features are instantiated for each gene by extracting localization 111 

annotations from both UniProt and Reactome. 112 

The third set of source and target features provides an embedded representation of 113 

the Gene Ontology (GO) annotations for each gene. We adopt the same approach used 114 

in GEARS ( 9) to construct a graph representing gene-gene functional relationships, and 115 

then infer an embedded representation of each node in this graph. Let Nu be the set of 116 

GO annotations for gene u. The Jaccard index between a pair of genes u, v can be 117 

calculated by Ju,v = |Nu∩Nv ||Nu∪Nv | , measuring the fraction of shared annotations between two 118 

genes. A GO graph is constructed by selecting the top k genes with highest values of 119 

Ju,v for each gene u, and adding a directed edge from u to each of these genes. We then 120 

apply node2vec (8; 16) to the GO graph and represent each gene by a 64-dimensional 121 

vector resulting from the learned node2vec embedding. We add to this representation a 122 

binary feature indicating the gene’s existence in the GO graph, and in cases in which a 123 

gene is not present in the graph (due to an absence of GO annotations), the 124 

64-dimensional vector is set to all zero values. 125 

When a phenotype is associated with multiple target nodes, the target feature vector 126 

is determined by aggregating the vectors derived for the individual target nodes. The 127 

cellular abundance and the GO features are aggregated by taking element-wise averages 128 

across the target nodes. The subcellular localization features are aggregated by taking 129 

the element-wise OR of the binary values in these features. 130 

The source-target relation features are intended to represent various aspects of how 131 

the source gene node and the target nodes are related in the knowledge graph. One 132 

subset of these features captures the level of evidence supporting a path between the 133 

source node and a target node. Specifically, for a path of length k, we extract an 134 

n-gram of length k that itemizes the strongest level of evidence supporting each 135 

interaction in the path. Interactions in the STRING physical network are labeled using 136 

three evidence categories: those annotated with ’E’ are collected from biochemical, 137 

biophysical and genetic interaction experiments, those annotated with ’D’ are curated 138 

from pathway databases, and those annotated with ’T’ are obtained by performing 139 

statistical co-occurrence analysis across the scientific literature. As an example, for a 140 

path consisting of three interactions with the strongest level of evidence supporting each 141 

being experimental, database, and experimental, respectively, we would extract the 142 

n-gram ’EDE’. The e(g→P ) vector includes an element for each such n-gram up to a 143 

maximum length. These features are set to counts that tally the n-gram occurrences for 144 

all paths between the source and a target node up to a maximum length, and then 145 

normalized to [0,1] using min-max scaling. 146 

Each interaction in STRING is also associated with a “combined score” indicating 147 

confidence in the interaction given all sources of evidence. We compute a feature 148 

representing the product of these scores for the most confident path. Denoting a path 149

abundance. We compile published experimental results from genome-scale CRISPR 182 

screens available in the literature, select target proteins for each phenotype, and 183 

construct data sets tailored for machine learning methods. An instance in one of our 184 

data sets is defined as a source-phenotype pair, where the source is a perturbed gene. 185 

We only consider instances for which the perturbed gene exists in the knowledge graph. 186

## Model Training 217 

In this study, we apply four machine-learning algorithms to learn models to predict the 218 

effects of gene perturbations: elastic net logistic regression (25), random forest (26), 219 

XGBoost ( 27 ), and neural networks (NN). We set the maximum of number of iterations 220 

to 5000 for elastic net, and the number of trees to 1,000 for random forest. Due to the 221 

imbalance of our data sets, we use balanced class weights during training of elastic net 222 

logistic regression, random forest, and XGBoost. 223 

The neural networks are fully connected between layers, and consist of one input 224 

layer, either one or two hidden layers, and one output unit. The hidden units use either 225 

the rectified linear unit (ReLU) activation function or the sigmoid activation function. 226 

After each hidden layer, a dropout layer is used with a dropout rate 0.3. The output unit 227 

uses a sigmoid activation function, and the loss function is binary cross-entropy loss. 228 

A set of hyperparameters for each alogrithm are tuned using internal 5-fold stratified 229 

cross-validation within each training set. The hyperparameters are tuned using a grid 230 

search over a manually-defined parameter space with the default value of the 231 

hyperparameter covered. For elastic net logistic regression, we tune the inverse of 232 

regularization strength (C), and the elastic net mixing parameter L1. For random forest, 233 

we tune the maximum tree depth, minimum number of samples for internal node 234 

splitting, and minimum number of samples for a leaf node. For XGBoost, we tune the 235 

learning rate, maximum tree depth, minimum sum of instance weight for a child, 236 

subsampling ratio of training set, subsampling ratio of features for each tree, and L1 237 

regulariztion on weights. For neural networks, we tune batch size, learning rate, number 238 

of training epochs, size of the hidden layer, number of the hidden layers, and the 239 

activation function used by the hidden layers. We allow the neural networks to have 240 

either one or two hidden layers, with all units using either a sigmoid or ReLU activation 241 

function. For two-layer neural networks, we set the size of the second hidden layer to be 242 

a quarter of the first hidden layer, and the first hidden layer has the same size range as 243 

the hidden layer in single-layer neural networks. 244

of these baselines are aimed at quantifying how “close” a source node is to a target node 248

For all three diffusion methods, we apply two different approaches that vary in how 265

approach, which we refer to as positive diffusion , the elements of p0 that correspond to 269 

the positive instances in the training set, in addition to the targets, are initialized to 1, 270 

and all other elements are set to 0. We can think of this diffusion process as starting 271 

from known positive source genes in addition to the targets for the phenotype. 272 

# Results 273 

In this section, we present a set of experiments designed to evaluate the predictive 274

## Learned models show high predictive accuracy 276 

We first test the ability of the learned models to predict the effects of held-aside gene 277 

perturbations on the phenotypes of interest by performing 5-fold cross-validation within 278 

each data set. For the phenotypes cholesterol homeostasis, cholesterol uptake, and 279 

influenza A virus replication, the cross validation is done using stratified sampling so 280 

that each fold contains approximately the same number of positive and negative 281 

instances. Each training set also includes a special instance for each target node in 282 

which the target itself is treated as the source node, given that we know that the target 283 

nodes are always important for the phenotype. For the mitochondrial protein 284 

abundance data set, we divide the 115 knocked-out genes into five folds so that each 285 

fold contains the same number of source genes. We construct ROC curves by pooling 286 

the test-set predictions across the folds for each data set. 287 

We learn models by applying four machine learning methods – elastic net logistic 288 

regression, random forest, XGBoost, and neural networks – to each data set. For all 289 

four phenotypes, we use internal 5-fold cross-validation within each training set to select 290 

the hyperparameters for that training set. 291

length and the target diffusion baselines for all phenotypes. Recall that the target 322 

diffusion approaches start the diffusion process only from the target nodes. 323 

Third, the highest predictive accuracy is achieved by a learned model in three out of 324 

the four phenotypes. However, the RWR positive diffusion and the heat positive 325 

diffusion baselines demonstrate competitive predictive accuracy for most phenotypes 326 

and superior accuracy for the influenza A virus replication phenotype. Recall that the 327 

positive diffusion approaches make use of a training set by starting the diffusion process 328 

from positive instances in the training set in addition to the target nodes. The relatively 329 

high predictive accuracy of these two baselines suggests that the genes that are involved 330 

in these phenotypes may be in close proximity in the knowledge graph, and thus 331 

especially amenable to prediction via diffusion. 332 

We note again that the diffusion baselines are advantaged in this analysis because we 333 

report the best predictive performance over a range of hyperparameter settings for each. 334 

Moreover, we note that a significant limitation of the the positive diffusion methods 335 

relative to our approach is that they cannot generalize to other phenotypes without 336 

having training sets specifically for those phenotypes. In contrast, our approach can be 337 

applied to make predictions for the effects of perturbations on phenotypes that were not 338 

represented in the training set. We explore this setting in detail in a subsequent section. 339 

Overall, we conclude that our learning approach provides a way to predict the impact of 340 

gene perturbations on a phenotype with a high degree of accuracy and generality. 341

# Discussion 464 

We have proposed a knowledge graph-based and learning method-agnostic approach 465 

that can predict the effects of gene perturbations on phenotypes of interest. The core 466 

idea underlying our approach is that the regularities of which gene perturbations will 467 

influence a phenotype can be elicited from a knowledge graph that incorporates 468 

representations of the gene, the phenotype, and the ways in which they are related via 469 

other entities and relationships in the graph. Our knowledge graph is assembled from 470 

diverse sources of evidence including protein-protein interactions, cellular abundances of 471 

gene products, protein subcellular localizations, and Gene Ontology annotations. 472 

We conducted an empirical evaluation applying four machine-learning methods to 473 

four data sets, each of which characterizes the effects of gene knockouts on a phenotype 474

high levels of predictive accuracy across the four, diverse phenotypes, (2) have better 476 

predictive accuracy than several standard baselines, (3) can often learn accurate models 477 

with small training sets, (4) benefit from having multiple sources of evidence in the 478 

input representation, (5) can, in many cases, transfer their predictive value to other 479 

phenotypes. Additionally, we showed that our approach is not especially sensitive to 480 

how we define negative instances, or to how a phenotype is represented as a set of target 481 

nodes. 482 

The key limitations of our approach are twofold. First, there is much room for 483 

improvement in the accuracy of the predictions the models make. We posit that this is 484 

due, in part, to noise in the genome-wide screens and their propensity to output many 485 

false negatives (24). However, we hypothesize that the predictive accuracy of the 486 

approach can be improved by augmenting the knowledge graph and by training models 487 

with more data. A second limitation of the approach is that it assumes that a 488 

phenotype can be represented by a set of surrogate target nodes in the knowledge graph. 489 

There are several directions that we will pursue in future research to address the 490 

limitations of our approach. First, we plan to expand the types of interactions and 491 

attributes included in the knowledge graph. For example, transcriptional regulatory 492 

relationships and gene orthology information are additional types of evidence that we 493 

could include. Second, we plan to assemble additional data sets for training and 494 

evaluating models. Third, we plan to investigate extensions to our approach for 495 

handling phenotypes that do not obviously map to a small number of target nodes. One 496 

idea here is to extend the graph to incorporate process or pathway nodes. Fourth, we 497 

will investigate the utility of interpretability methods for gaining mechanistic insight 498 

into the relationships between genes and the phenotypes they influence. Fifth, we plan 499 

to adapt the approach to learn models based on graph neural networks. We believe that 500 

these extensions will advance this work towards the goal of being able to predict the 501 

effects of arbitrary gene perturbations on virtually any molecular or cellular phenotype. 502
