Abstract 8

T cells must co-recognize peptide and HLA, yet the extent to which this specificity is 9

shaped by germline-encoded TCR–HLA contacts versus selection during thymic development has 10 

remained difficult to quantify. Leveraging population-scale TCR β repertoires linked to donor HLA 11 

genotypes, we construct allele-specific V β usage profiles and normalize them to repertoire-wide 12 

baselines to derive interpretable HLA-TCR V β preference vectors. We demonstrate that different 13 

HLA alleles imprint distinct biases in the usage frequency of TCR V β gene segments among 14 

the public TCRs that engage those alleles; consistent with germline-encoded TCR–HLA contact 15 

preferences, certain V β genes are over-represented for particular HLA alleles. Similarities in HLA 16 

amino-acid sequence predict similarities in both their V β preferences and peptide-binding motifs; 17 

a residue-level analysis disentangles HLA positions primarily associated with TCR engagement 18 

from those associated with peptide motifs. The TCR-associated HLA positions localize to 19 

canonical TCR-facing helices, whereas peptide-associated HLA positions track binding pockets, 20 

revealing distinct molecular routes by which HLA polymorphism shapes the TCR and peptide 21 

sides of recognition. CMV exposure stratifications confirmed these patterns are not explained 22 

by a few dominant infections. Together, these data support that germline-encoded constraints 23 

set the landscape of TCR–HLA compatibility, while thymic and peptide-driven forces tune the 24 

realized repertoire. These HLA-specific V β biases are a biological prior that should provide a 25 

baseline for better understanding of TCR-pHLA specificity as a whole and should be accounted 26 

for in future evaluation of any TCR-pHLA specificity prediction methods. 27

## 1 Introduction 30 

Interactions between T cell receptors (TCRs) and peptide-presenting Human Leukocyte Antigens 31 

(HLAs) are central to adaptive immunity, enabling the immune system to distinguish self from non-self 32

and mount targeted responses to pathogens, cancer and other immunological threats (Murphy and 33 

Weaver, 2017a). A deeper understanding of this interaction could transform our ability to predict 34 

immune responses, design vaccines, personalize immunotherapies and uncover the pathogenesis of 35 

autoimmune disorders. Achieving this will likely require a precise understanding of how TCRs engage 36 

with peptide–HLA (pHLA) complexes at the molecular level. Despite substantial progress (Davis 37 

and Bjorkman, 1988; Rudolph et al., 2006; Rossjohn et al., 2015; Murphy and Weaver, 2017b), key 38 

aspects of this molecular interaction remain incompletely understood. 39 

Understanding TCR–pHLA interactions is complicated by their extraordinary complexity. HLAs 40 

are among the most polymorphic human genes, with tens of thousands of alleles identified across the 41 

six classical class I (HLA-A, -B, -C) and II loci (HLA-DR, -DQ and -DP) (Klein and Sato, 2000). 42 

Structurally, the HLA peptide-binding groove is defined by two helices: in Class I the helices are 43 

named α1 and α2 and are part of the same polypeptide chain, while for Class 2 the groove is formed 44 

by two distinct chains, α1 and β1. In the canonical binding mode, the TCR β interacts with around 45 

the α1 helix in Class I and the β1 helix in Class II (See Figure5 A-D) Christopher Garcia et al. 46 

(2009); Cohn et al. (2019)). 47 

Polymorphisms are concentrated in the peptide-binding groove, influencing both peptide presen-48 

tation and TCR binding (Tiercy, 2002; Nakamura et al., 2019; Karnaukhov et al., 2022). Moreover, 49 

TCR binding is degenerate: a single TCR can recognize many pHLAs and many TCRs can recognize 50 

the same pHLA (Davis and Bjorkman, 1988; Sewell, 2012; Murphy and Weaver, 2017b). While 51 

many predictive models use sequence-based, structural or molecular dynamics approaches (Springer 52 

et al., 2021; Grazioli et al., 2023; Bradley, 2023; Jensen and Nielsen, 2024), they often fail to general-53 

ize (Drost et al., 2025), limited by interaction complexity and sparsity of high-quality data (Grazioli 54 

et al., 2022; Castorina et al., 2025). In the absence of massive training sets, uncovering mechanistic 55 

principles can provide inductive biases that improve model generalizability. This strategy mirrors 56 

the success of AlphaFold, which leveraged Multiple Sequence Alignments to identify co-evolutionary 57 

constraints and guide structural prediction (Jumper et al., 2021). Similarly, statistical frameworks 58 

like ours may uncover residue-level features that constrain TCR–pHLA binding and inform the 59 

next generation of predictive models. This perspective is especially relevant given ongoing efforts to 60 

determine whether TCR specificity arises from germline-encoded features such as CDR1 and CDR2 61 

loops that may have evolved HLA specificity or is shaped entirely by thymic selection acting on 62 

random repertoires (La Gruta et al., 2018). Clarifying these mechanisms may guide the development 63 

of more general and interpretable models. 64 

Recent advances in sequencing technologies have enabled statistical approaches to examine how 65 

HLA polymorphism shapes TCR gene usage. Sharon et al. (2016) proposed that if germline-encoded 66 

contacts influence TCR–HLA interactions, then TCR V βs should show biased usage depending 67 

on HLA type. Supporting this, their trans-eQTL analysis found that polymorphisms near the 68 

HLA binding groove correlate with V β expression, linking HLA polymorphism to TCR repertoire 69 

composition. However, their use of bulk RNA sequencing averaged over the entire T cell compartment, 70 

potentially dilutes the HLA-specific signals. To improve resolution, we leverage a previously developed 71

unseen populations, yielding TCR β sets with strong evidence of true immunological specificity. 76 

Using HLA-associated TCR β sets, we quantify V β gene usage for each HLA and examine 77 

how these patterns correlate with HLA polymorphism, while statistically accounting for potential 78 

mediation by shared peptide motifs. We identify HLA residues associated with V β usage, peptide 79 

repertoire or both. To compare patterns across HLA types, we harmonize sequence and structural 80 

data through sequence alignments and 3D structure mapping. Although our analysis is limited to 81 

the TCR β chain, it reveals population-level trends consistent with germline influences and highlights 82 

candidate HLA residues that may shape TCR–HLA specificity through direct structural mechanisms. 83 

The scale of this analysis enables resolution of TCR–HLA associations not previously accessible, 84 

offering biologically grounded priors to inform predictive models. 85 

## 2 Materials and Methods 86 

2.1 HLAdb 87 

Using direct HLA genotyping and 4,144 TCR β repertoires, Zahid et al. (2025a) built sensitive and 88 

specific HLA imputation models from TCR βs associated with hundreds of common HLAs. We 89 

impute 145 of the most common HLAs for 30,000 subjects in the T-Detect COVID cohort (Zahid 90 

et al., 2025b). These imputed HLAs were used as case-control labels and TCR βs were statistically 91 

associated to HLAs using via a one-sided Fisher’s Exact Test following Zahid et al. (2025a). By 92 

imputing HLAs on a set of repertoires that are nearly an order of magnitude larger in sample size 93 

than the original data set used to build the models, we are able to leverage the genotyped labels 94 

available for a smaller set of repertoires and the statistical power of sequence discovery offered by 95 

the larger cohort. 96 

We restricted analysis to HLAs with more than 5,000 associated public TCR βs. In total, 2,827,857 97 

public TCR V β sequences from 41 unique public TCR V β genes associated with 91 HLA alleles 98 

were included in this analysis (see Supplementary Table S1). Using the set of TCR βs associated 99 

with each HLA, we generate the TCR V β usage pattern as the distribution of V β gene preferences 100 

for that particular HLA. V β genes are not uniformly represented in the TCR β repertoire, thus we 101 

normalize V β gene usage to the background frequency measured from all TCR βs (public and private) 102 

summed across repertoires and this is represented as a vector (see Supplementary Figure S2 and 103 

Supplementary Table S1). 104

2.3 Pairwise Distance Analysis of HLA 118 

The TCR-pHLA interaction involves the TCR, the HLA and a peptide. We characterize HLA 119 

differences with three pairwise distance matrices: 120 

• HLA polymorphism : BLOSUM62 distance between HLA sequences (See 2.3.1). 121 

• Vβ gene preference change : cosine distance between V β preference vectors (See 2.3.2). 122 

• Peptide motif change : Jensen–Shannon (JS) divergence between Position Weight Matrix 123 

(PWM) (See 2.3.3). 124 

We then correlate these matrices to identify HLA positions whose variation is associated with 125 

TCR β usage or peptide motifs, disentangling TCR–HLA from pHLA effects. 126 

We analyse both within HLA loci and between loci. Within-locus analyses aim to identify 127 

binding-groove positions most significant for the peptide or the TCR β.128 

Between-locus analyses aim to identify sequence positions that distinguish loci and correlate 129 

with the TCR β or the peptide motifs. 130

To better understand how germline-encoded TCR features and HLA polymorphisms shape pHLA 205

Our goal is to disentangle the contributions of direct TCR–HLA interactions from those arising 208 

indirectly through variation in peptide presentation. 209 

Our analysis draws on large-scale repertoire sequencing data linking TCR βs with HLAs across 210 

a cohort of approximately 30,000 individuals (see Methods Section 2.1). V β gene usage patterns 211 

for common HLAs are derived from TCR βs statistically associated with each HLA and aggregated 212 

into vectors of normalized frequencies (Supplementary Figure S2), which we interpret as V β gene 213 

preferences of each HLA. To validate that these patterns are stable and not confounded by individual 214 

immune exposure, we compare repertoires from Cytomegalovirus (CMV) -positive and CMV-negative 215 

subjects, restricting analysis to HLA-associated TCRs observed in individuals carrying the relevant 216 

HLA allele. We use CMV exposure as a test case as it typically has a strong impact on the 217 

T cell repertoire (Klenerman and Oxenius, 2016) and can be inferred directly from the TCR β218 

repertoire (Emerson et al., 2017; May et al., 2024). This makes CMV a strong test case: if a 219 

single dominant exposure perturbs V β usage patterns, we should detect it in CMV-positive subjects. 220 

As shown in Figure 2, V β usage is consistent between CMV-positive and CMV-negative subjects, 221 

supporting the conclusion that V β gene preferences reflect HLA-driven selection rather than exposure-222 

dependent biases. Using the T-Detect COVID cohort (Zahid et al., 2025b) of approximately 30,000 223 

individuals, we infer both CMV exposure status (Emerson et al., 2017) and HLA genotype (Zahid 224 

et al., 2025a). We focus on individuals carrying HLA-A02:01, comparing V β usage among CMV-225 

positive (N = 5,449) and CMV-negative (N = 8,136) subjects. For each person, we quantify V β usage 226 

across all HLA-A02:01–associated TCR β sequences in their repertoire (45,737 unique sequences). We 227 

observe no significant difference in V β usage patterns between the CMV-positive and CMV-negative 228 

groups (see Figure 2). Restricting the analysis to other HLAs yields consistent results. 229 

Although this analysis does not rule out contributions from other antigen exposures, it shows 230 

that even a strong immune stimulus like CMV does not measurably influence HLA-associated V β231 

usage patterns. This supports the interpretation that the observed associations reflect generalizable, 232 

population-level trends. 233

3.1 Variation in V β Preference Breadth Across HLA Loci 250 

To assess how HLA polymorphism affects the breadth of the T cell repertoire, we quantified how 251 

“exclusive” the V β gene usage was for each HLA gene using Shannon entropy ( Hnorm ) on the 252 

conditional probability distribution P (HLA | Vβ). In the case of HLA-associated TCRs restricted 253 

to single V β gene, the entropy score would be close to 0. Conversely, uniform usage of all V β genes 254 

would yield a score closer to 1. 255 

As shown in Supplementary Figure S18, there is a significant difference in restriction breadth 256 

across loci. Class II loci HLA-DR and HLA-DP displayed normalized entropy values approaching 257 

the theoretical maximum ( Hnorm ≈ 0.99 ), corresponding to a nearly uniform distribution of V β258 

usage across alleles. In contrast, HLA-C exhibited a significantly lower mean entropy and higher 259 

variance compared to HLA-DR (see Supplementary Table S10). HLA-C shows the lowest entropy 260 

and highest variance across loci, indicating a high degree of restriction due to specific V β genes being 261 

associated with a limited subset of alleles. HLA-A, -B and -DQ display intermediate entropy profiles 262 

significantly distinct from both the HLA-C and HLA-DR/DP extremes (Supplementary Table S10) 263 

These differences highlight substantial locus specific variation in the overall permissiveness of 264 

Vβ gene usage. With this baseline heterogeneity in mind, we next examine how differences in HLA 265 

sequence relate to similarity in V β gene preferences and peptide binding across alleles. 266 

3.2 Global Correlations Across HLAs 267

structures. These results suggest that variation in HLA sequence alone is sufficient to predict 273 

meaningful trends in V β gene usage, highlighting the potential for sequence-based features to inform 274 

models of TCR–HLA specificity. 275

## 4 Discussion 344 

Our analysis leverages a large-scale statistical framework, using 30,000 TCR β repertoires to associate 345 

specific TCR βs with individual HLAs. By averaging across thousands of individuals and diverse 346 

but commonly shared antigenic exposures, we derive normalized V β gene preferences for each HLA, 347 

enabling a robust, population-level assessment of TCR β-HLA preferences. This approach yields 348 

sets of TCR βs that are statistically enriched for HLA specificity, providing statistical power to link 349 

TCR βs to their restricting HLAs despite the polygenic nature of HLA phenotypes. 350 

To understand how HLA polymorphism shapes peptide binding and TCR engagement, we 351 

analyze correlations between HLA sequence similarity, peptide motifs and V β gene usage across the 352 

cohort. Our goal is to distinguish direct TCR–HLA associations from peptide-mediated effects. We 353

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. find that HLA sequence similarity correlates with both V β gene usage and peptide motifs. These 354 

associations map to specific polymorphic residues linked to either V β preference, peptide features or 355 

both. Structurally, key positions cluster around the α1 helix in class I and the β1 helix in class II, 356 

with particularly strong signals in the DR locus. These results suggest that TCR–HLA specificity is 357 

shaped by a combination of germline-encoded contacts and indirect peptide-driven effects, including 358 

potential allosteric modulation of pHLA conformation. Together, these patterns highlight distinct 359 

structural contributions to recognition across HLA classes. Notably, the clustering of TCR-associated 360 

residues on the Class I α1 helix structurally confirms the germline contact maps proposed by Marrack 361 

et al. (2008). 362 

A longstanding question in immunology is whether TCR–pHLA specificity is governed more by 363 

inherited structural constraints or by developmental selection, reflecting broader questions about 364 

how evolutionary forces (Feng et al., 2007; Marrack et al., 2008; Christopher Garcia et al., 2009; 365 

Yin et al., 2012) and thymic processes (Van Laethem et al., 2007, 2012; Lu et al., 2019) shape 366 

the TCR repertoire. Evidence for intrinsic specificity includes recognition of pHLAs by randomly 367 

generated T cells (Krovi et al., 2019), QTL associations between HLAs and TCR V β usage (Sharon 368 

et al., 2016; Gao et al., 2019) and alloreactivity (Felix and Allen, 2007), where TCRs selected on 369 

self-HLAs cross-react with non-self HLAs. Our findings support this view. Namely, polymorphic 370 

residues, especially in the α1 helix of class I and the β1 helix of class II, correlate with V β gene 371 

preference but not peptide motifs, consistent with germline-encoded structural bias and allele-specific 372 

recognition (Cohn et al., 2019). We also identify residues associated only with peptide motifs 373 

and those associated with both peptide motifs and V β genes, indicating multiple mechanisms 374 

through which HLA polymorphism shapes TCR engagement. A parsimonious interpretation is that 375 

TCR–HLA interactions reflect both co-evolution and selection (Rangarajan and Mariuzza, 2014; 376 

La Gruta et al., 2018), with germline contacts priming initial binding and thymic selection, subject 377 

to these germline constraints, sculpting the final repertoire. This layered model helps reconcile 378 

competing hypotheses and offers a unified framework for understanding repertoire architecture. 379 

Our analysis suggests that V β restriction follows a continuum rather than a simple Class I 380 

versus Class II split. HLA-DR and HLA-DP appear to be “generalists” with high entropy and broad 381 

compatibility. On the other hand, HLA-C seems to act as a “specialist” with lower entropy compared 382 

to other HLAs. This complements Gao et al. (2019)’s observation that HLA-C has fewer TCR 383 

associations than other Class I loci. While they attribute this to low surface expression, our data 384 

implies that these associations are more specific for HLA-C than for other loci. Therefore, the weak 385 

HLA-C signal could result from a combination of low abundance and structural selectivity. This 386 

divergence in part reflects the functional needs of CD8+ T cells to have precise recognition to avoid 387 

autoimmunity, whereas CD4+ T cells benefit from broader scanning capabilities. 388 

Several findings raise compelling questions for future investigation. In class I HLAs, V β gene 389 

associations cluster near the α1 helix, which directly contacts the TCR β chain. In contrast, HLA-DR 390 

associations are exclusively driven by the polymorphic β chain (DRB), since the α chain (DRA) 391 

remains constant. This provides a residue-level mechanism for the HLA-DRB1 expression QTLs 392

The absence of similar correlations in HLA-DQ and -DP, where both chains are polymorphic, 396 

underscores the unique role of the invariant DRA chain in shaping recognition. Additionally, we 397 

identify HLA positions with negative correlations to TCR β or peptide similarity, potentially reflecting 398 

compensatory or co-evolutionary effects that preserve recognition despite sequence divergence. These 399 

patterns suggest novel structural mechanisms of immune recognition and point to the value of 400 

follow-up studies using mutagenesis, structural modeling or biophysical assays. 401 

Despite the statistical power and scale of our framework, several limitations should guide 402 

interpretation. Our analysis focuses exclusively on the TCR β chain and does not account for 403 

potential contributions from the TCR α chain, which also engages the pHLA surface and may exhibit 404 

its own germline biases. Likewise, our emphasis on HLA polymorphism may overlook conserved 405 

germline-encoded residues that play critical roles in TCR recognition. While we focus on V β406 

gene–encoded CDR1 and CDR2 regions, we do not account for the hypervariable CDR3 loop, which 407 

is central to peptide recognition and also influences HLA binding. In addition, peptide repertoires 408 

used to define motif similarity are drawn from public datasets often biased toward disease-associated 409 

antigens, potentially limiting their generalizability. Furthermore, some observed correlations may 410 

arise from linkage disequilibrium between HLA alleles, rather than from direct structural contacts. 411 

Conversely, the absence of correlation does not imply a lack of interaction but may reflect limited 412 

power, resolution or data sparsity. Structural modeling and molecular dynamics simulations could 413 

help resolve these ambiguities by assessing the functional impact of specific residues. Finally, as 414 

high-throughput technologies improve, future studies incorporating paired TCR αβ repertoires and 415 

more representative peptide libraries will be essential to validate and expand these insights. 416 

A key assumption of our analysis is that the structural associations identified in the public 417 

repertoire reflect fundamental biophysical constraints applicable to all TCRs. Under this assumption, 418 

the V β–HLA associations we identify provide important constraints for modeling TCR-pHLA 419 

interactions. The P (V β | HLA ) and P (HLA | V β ) matrices provide simple priors that can bias a 420 

model toward germline-consistent TCRs when assigning HLAs or ranking candidate receptors. This 421 

is particularly useful in low-data settings (e.g. rare HLAs or small clinical cohorts), where these 422 

priors could help prevent models from overfitting to noise. These priors can also be incorporated 423 

into benchmarking via a simple prior-weighted accuracy metric. 424 

Our findings support a model of TCR–HLA specificity shaped by both inherited constraints 425 

and adaptive processes. Germline-encoded biases appear to define the baseline potential for V β426 

engagement with HLA, while thymic selection and immune experience further sculpt the repertoire. 427 

By leveraging tens of thousands of repertoires to derive V β usage patterns and mapping these onto 428 

HLA sequence and structure, we perform a residue-level analysis that provides insights into the 429 

molecular basis of TCR–HLA interactions. Our approach demonstrates the power of population-430 

scale, structure-aware statistical analysis to uncover fundamental principles of immune recognition. 431
