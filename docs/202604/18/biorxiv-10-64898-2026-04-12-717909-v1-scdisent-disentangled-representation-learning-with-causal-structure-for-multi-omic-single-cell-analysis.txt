# scDisent: disentangled representation learning with causal structure for multi-omic single-cell analysis 

Guoren Xi 

Datenintegrationszentrum, Universitätsklinikum Jena Correspondence: xiguoren@hotmail.com 

Abstract 

Single-cell multi-omic technologies measure complementary aspects of cellular identity and regulatory state, yet most integration models compress these signals into one entangled latent space. Such representations are useful for clustering but poorly suited for mechanistic inter-pretation or perturbation-oriented analysis. We present scDisent ( https://github.com/xig uoren/scDisent ), a generative framework for disentangled representation learning that sep-arates expression-associated variables ( zexpr ) from regulation-associated variables ( zreg ) and links them through a sparse directed mapping. scDisent combines modality-specific encoding, variational disentanglement with total-correlation and orthogonality constraints, and a Gumbel-gated causal module protected by detach-based gradient isolation. Evaluated on benchmark datasets with matched modalities, scDisent achieved best-in-benchmark integration performance while exposing regulatory structure that competing integration methods do not model explicitly. The learned causal atlas remained sparse, perturbation analyses recovered biologically coherent lineage-associated programs, and cross-dataset discovery analyses highlighted interpretable im-mune, neural and developmental signatures. Quantitative branch-separation analyses further showed that benchmark-label information concentrated in zexpr rather than zreg . Together, these results position scDisent as a computational method that improves not only integration quality but also biological interpretability, making single-cell multi-omic representations better suited to biological question answering and in silico hypothesis generation. 

## 1 Introduction 

Recent single-cell multi-omic technologies make it possible to observe complementary molecular layers within the same cell [1–5]. When multiple modalities are measured for the same population, these data allow one to ask not only how cells cluster, but also which regulatory states may underlie those transcriptional identities. This experimental design has motivated a large class of integration methods that aim to denoise, align, cluster, and embed cells into a unified latent representation [6–14]. In practice, these methods are highly effective for cell-state discovery, but they usually optimize for a single mixed latent space that merges cellular state, lineage identity, and regulatory variation into one representation. That entanglement creates a limitation. If the same latent coordinates simultaneously encode both cell identity and putative regulatory drivers, then modifying those coordinates does not cor-respond to a clean biological intervention. This matters most when the question is not only whether populations separate, but which regulatory programs distinguish neighboring lineages or cell states. This limitation appears across method families. Variational generative models such as scVI and MultiVI, factor-analysis frameworks such as MOFA+ and LIGER, and embedding or alignment methods such as Cobolt, scJoint, Harmony, scGLUE and WNN all provide useful multimodal summaries or strong integration baselines [6, 8–12, 15, 16, 18, 19]. Translation-oriented models such as BABEL and Polarbear are also powerful for missing-modality prediction and cross-modal alignment [20, 21]. What these approaches generally do not provide is an explicit split between identity-preserving and regulation-oriented factors linked by a directed interface. We address this problem with scDisent, a disentangled causal representation model for single-cell multi-omic analysis. The model partitions the latent space into an expression branch, zexpr , and a regulatory branch, zreg . The expression branch is encouraged to capture stable cell-state informa-tion, whereas the regulatory branch encodes modulatory factors that map toward the expression branch through a sparse causal layer. scDisent combines total-correlation reduction and orthogo-nality constraints [22] with a detach-based gradient barrier that prevents the causal objective from corrupting the expression manifold. The motivation of the paper is therefore broader than another benchmark gain. Current multi-omic models still lack a representation that distinguishes cellular identity from regulatory hypothe-ses [13, 14, 17]. If that distinction is made explicit, one model can support clustering, lineage-aware interpretation and perturbation-oriented analysis within a single framework. This work makes three contributions. First, we introduce a dual-branch latent architecture that separates expression identity from regulatory variation. Second, we couple these branches with a sparse causal mapping layer that provides an explicit regulatory-to-expression interface while preserving expression geometry through gradient isolation. Third, we show on benchmark datasets with matched modalities that this design improves both performance and interpretability, enabling biologically coherent perturbation hypotheses rather than only better clustering [14, 17]. 

## 2 Methods 

2.1 Problem formulation 

Let Xrna ∈ Rn×p and Xatac ∈ Rn×q denote two molecular views of the same cells in the paired benchmark setting studied here. Each training example contains matched modalities from the same cell. Rather than learning a single joint latent space, scDisent separates two biological roles that are usually mixed together in conventional embeddings: 

z = [ zexpr ; zreg ],

p(Xatac | zexpr , z reg ). A directed mapping from zreg to zexpr makes the regulatory branch readable in terms of its influence on the expression branch. The central hypothesis is that this factorization remains useful even when the model is trained on observational data alone [14, 17]. Under this view, zexpr remains the main carrier of cell identity, whereas zreg organizes residual regulatory variation into a form that supports regulatory attribution, in silico perturbation and lineage-aware hypothesis generation. 

2.1.1 Modeling targets 

The objective has three parts: reconstruction of both modalities, non-trivial separation between expression-preserving and regulation-oriented information, and a directional constraint that makes the regulatory branch readable in terms of its influence on zexpr .

2.1.2 Generative view 

From a probabilistic perspective, scDisent remains a multimodal variational model in which both observed modalities are generated from the concatenated latent state, with an additional directional constraint between the two latent branches: 

p(Xrna , X atac , z expr , z reg ) = p(zexpr ) p(zreg ) p(Xrna | zexpr , z reg ) p(Xatac | zexpr , z reg ),

with Gaussian priors on the latent branches. The sparse mapping from zreg to zexpr does not replace this factorization; it adds a representation-level inductive bias designed to make regulation-oriented variation more interpretable. scDisent therefore does not claim direct causal identification from observational data, but couples multimodal reconstruction with a latent geometry better suited to biological hypothesis generation [14, 17]. 

2.2 Datasets 

We evaluated scDisent on three benchmark datasets spanning immune and neural contexts: PBMC 10k, Human Brain 3k, and Mouse E18 [23–25]. All three provide paired observations at the cell level. Together they cover mature immune lineages, heterogeneous brain populations and develop-mental trajectories. 2.3 Baselines 

We compared scDisent against four representative baselines spanning major method families in single-cell integration and multi-omic analysis. scVI serves as an RNA-only variational reference [18]. MultiVI extends that framework to multimodal integration and missing-modality settings [15]. scGLUE provides a graph-guided deep-learning comparator [16]. Seurat weighted-nearest-neighbor analysis provides a widely used non-generative baseline [19]. The benchmark therefore mixes RNA-only and multimodal references. None of these baselines explicitly separates a regulation-oriented branch from an identity-preserving expression branch. 

2.3.1 Baseline limitations in the context of scDisent 

The comparison is therefore not only about clustering score, but also about what kind of biological question the learned representation can support. Variational references such as scVI and MultiVI provide expressive latent manifolds, but direct manipulation of those manifolds has no explicit semantics for preserving identity while varying regulation. scGLUE still optimizes a joint repre-sentation rather than a two-branch decomposition with a directional interface, and WNN is an effective statistical integrator rather than a mechanistic latent model. These methods can succeed at integration while remaining less suited to perturbation-oriented regulatory interpretation. 

2.4 Model architecture 

scDisent is an end-to-end generative model with four components: modality-specific encoders, a dual-branch disentanglement head, a sparse regulatory-to-expression mapping, and modality de-coders. In the unified benchmark configuration, the model uses dexpr = 128 and dreg = 128 with shared hidden dimensions (1024 , 512 , 256) and dropout 0.1 throughout the encoder and decoder backbones. 

2.4.1 Dual-modal encoders

The ATAC branch takes the 20,000 most variable peaks and, when available, appends gene-activity features to the peak matrix before encoding, following common single-cell chromatin analysis practice [26, 27]. 2.4.4 Causal mapping layer 

The model further includes a sparse linear mapping from the regulatory branch to a predicted expression branch: 

ˆzexpr = zreg (W ⊙ G),

where W ∈ Rdreg ×dexpr contains effect sizes and G is a binary gate matrix sampled with a straight-through Gumbel-Softmax estimator [29]. During training, each edge uses a learnable on/off logit and temperature τ = 1 .0; during evaluation, edges are thresholded at 0.5. This design gives each potential regulatory-to-expression edge an interpretable discrete selection mechanism while remaining differentiable during optimization. 2.4.5 Parameter freezing across phases 

The optimization schedule also imposes architectural roles through parameter freezing. In phase 1 and phase 2, parameters of the causal map are frozen, so the encoders, disentanglement head and decoders must first establish a stable multimodal representation before any directed regulatory prediction is learned. Only in phase 3 is the causal layer unfrozen for end-to-end fine-tuning. This detail matters because it prevents the sparse mapping from absorbing noise too early and makes the final causal atlas easier to interpret.

2.7 Phased training schedule 

Training is staged into three phases totaling 350 epochs. Phase 1 lasts 50 epochs and optimizes reconstruction while keeping the causal layer frozen. Phase 2 lasts 150 epochs and activates KL, total-correlation, orthogonality, contrastive alignment and the auxiliary zexpr -only decoder, while the causal layer remains frozen. Phase 3 lasts another 150 epochs and unfreezes the entire model so that the causal map is optimized jointly with the encoders and decoders. The training schedule is designed to match the semantic intent of the architecture. Phase 1 establishes stable modality-specific reconstruction and prevents the model from trying to learn directed regulatory structure before it has learned a reasonable shared multimodal state. Phase 2 encourages the latent split itself by introducing KL, total-correlation, orthogonality, contrastive alignment and the auxiliary expression-only decoder. Only in phase 3 is the regulatory branch asked to explain part of the expression branch through the sparse mapping layer. Two warmup mechanisms are used to reduce optimization shocks. 2.7.1 Phase-specific optimization logic 

The three phases progressively increase model constraints. Phase 1 learns a stable multimodal reconstruction model. Phase 2 encourages partial independence of the two branches. Phase 3 asks whether the structured regulatory branch can explain part of the expression manifold through a sparse directed interface. This sequencing yielded more stable optimization and more interpretable causal atlases than exposing all objectives at full strength from the start. 

2.8 Evaluation protocol 

For fair comparison, all methods were evaluated on the same processed benchmark datasets and scored using a standardized Leiden clustering sweep over thirteen resolutions from 0.1 to 2.0 [34]. We report ARI and NMI as primary metrics and ASW as a cluster compactness reference. Because several baselines achieve their best ARI at different resolutions, the shared sweep avoids resolution-selection bias. We additionally assess biological interpretability through causal-atlas inspection, lineage-specific perturbation analysis, external resource cross-checking, and quantitative branch separation. 

2.8.1 Primary evaluation metrics 

The main quantitative benchmark uses adjusted Rand index (ARI) and normalized mutual infor-mation (NMI) between Leiden clusters and reference benchmark labels [35, 36]. In this study, those 10

2.8.2 Benchmark philosophy 

This benchmark evaluates more than whether a model produces visually plausible embeddings. Clustering metrics test whether scDisent preserves cell-state geometry; disentanglement summaries test whether branch semantics are separated; and causal-atlas and perturbation analyses test whether the regulatory branch can be used for hypothesis-oriented downstream analysis. Together these readouts match the scientific claim of the paper more closely than clustering metrics alone. 

## 3 Results 

3.1 Overview of the experimental narrative 

The results address three linked questions: whether scDisent remains competitive for integration across immune, neural and developmental datasets; which architectural components account for that gain; and whether the resulting representation supports biological questions that are difficult to ask in entangled latent spaces. Benchmark performance, ablation logic and discovery analyses are therefore presented as mutually supporting evidence that branch-aware representation learning improves both integration and biological interpretability [13, 17]. 

3.2 scDisent resolves cell identity across immune and neural benchmark datasets 

scDisent remained competitive as an integrative model across all three benchmarks and achieved the strongest ARI in every dataset tested. The gain was clearest in PBMC 10k, where scDisent reached ARI 0.627 and NMI 0.610. The same configuration remained competitive in Human Brain 3k (ARI 0.521, NMI 0.626) and Mouse E18 (ARI 0.313, NMI 0.387), indicating that the model does not rely on one tissue-specific operating point. This consistency matters because the three benchmarks differ substantially in biological geom-etry. PBMC contains relatively mature immune lineages, whereas Human Brain and Mouse E18 contain subtler neural and developmental structure [38–41]. The complete resolution sweeps further show that the gain is not driven by one favorable clustering threshold, but persists across a shared evaluation grid. 11

3.3 Latent visualizations separate identity-preserving and regulatory structure 

Latent visualizations support the intended semantic split between the two branches. The expres-sion branch preserves recognizable lineage geometry, whereas the regulatory branch is more diffuse and therefore less redundant with coarse population separation. In PBMC, lymphoid and myeloid populations remain sharply organized in zexpr , while zreg retains heterogeneity without simply repro-ducing the same clustering pattern [42, 43]. Supplementary baseline UMAPs show that competing methods can recover reasonable manifolds, but they do not provide the same explicit separation between identity-preserving and regulation-oriented structure. The baseline UMAP panels sharpen this point. Across PBMC, Human Brain and Mouse E18, scVI, MultiVI, scGLUE and WNN all return single manifolds that are useful for neighborhood structure and coarse population recovery. 3.8 scDisent enables biological questions that are not native to standard inte-gration models 

Across all three datasets, the main added value of scDisent is not that it replaces annotation or differential expression analysis, but that it introduces a branch-aware representational logic. Once expression-associated and regulation-associated variation are separated, one can ask which latent regulators are enriched in a lineage or state and which expression programs are most sensitive to perturbation of those regulators. These questions are difficult to phrase cleanly in standard joint latent spaces, because perturbing the manifold simultaneously changes identity, regulatory context and reconstruction behavior [15, 16, 19].

3.10 Sensitivity analysis supports the default configuration as a robust operat-ing point 

Sensitivity scans across latent dimensionality and loss weights showed that the default configuration is close to a strong compromise across datasets. ## 4 Discussion 

Most current multi-omic single-cell methods are optimized for integration quality, not for mecha-nistic decomposition [15, 16, 19]. They can align modalities and recover cell populations, but they do so with entangled latent spaces that are difficult to manipulate or interpret. scDisent instead changes the structure of the representation itself by separating identity-like expression variables from regulatory variables and coupling them through a sparse directed interface. The combined results support this view from three directions. First, scDisent remains compet-itive across immune, neural and developmental benchmarks, so it is not trading performance for interpretability. Second, the PBMC ablations show that the main gain comes from enforcing branch semantics, with disentanglement producing the largest drop when removed and detach-based pro-tection stabilizing the interaction between the two branches. Third, the perturbation outputs and sparse causal atlas show that the regulatory branch can still be read in biologically meaningful ways after training. Existing models may succeed at building an integrated manifold, but they do not naturally provide a branch of the representation that can be read as a candidate regulatory interface [8, 10, 12, 15, 16]. 

4.1 Relationship to current multi-omic modeling practice 

These results also help place scDisent within the current landscape of multi-omic modeling. Meth-ods such as MultiVI, scGLUE and WNN were not built around the requirement that identity-preserving variables and perturbation-oriented regulatory variables should occupy different latent branches [15, 16, 19]. It is therefore unsurprising that they remain strong integration baselines while offering less explicit support for regulator-centered reasoning. The claim here is not that every single-cell analysis requires causal structure, but that once the goal shifts from cell-state summarization toward regulator prioritization or directional latent interpretation, representation semantics become central [14, 17]. 

4.2 From observational integration to perturbation-oriented modeling 

The conceptual shift of scDisent is that it represents multi-omic cells in a form better matched to perturbation-oriented questions, not only observational embedding. In standard joint latent spaces, latent manipulation tends to move cell identity and regulatory context simultaneously, which makes downstream intervention hard to interpret [8, 10, 15, 16]. In scDisent, the expression branch remains the anchor for identity-preserving geometry, whereas the regulatory branch is the place where perturbation hypotheses are read out. This does not make the model experimentally causal in the strict interventional sense, but it does make directional biological questions easier to pose. 18

4.3 Biological meaning of the causal layer 

The sparse regulatory atlas is useful precisely because it is not dense. A dense all-to-all coupling would offer little interpretive value and would be difficult to distinguish from a generic latent transformation. By contrast, the current PBMC atlas emphasizes a limited number of dominant links and produces lineage-restricted perturbation outputs. The additional DoRothEA cross-check on PBMC, recorded in results/dorothea_validation_summary.txt , further supports the claim that the perturbation signatures are not arbitrary [45], although the recurrence of the same best-matching TF means this should be interpreted as a coarse plausibility check rather than a one-to-one regulator identification. The most defensible interpretation is therefore that scDisent exposes a structured hypothesis space that can prioritize mechanistically plausible regulators for follow-up analysis. It does not re-place perturbation experiments, but it can help decide which perturbations are most worth testing. 

4.4 Computational tradeoffs and scope 

The added interpretability of scDisent is not free. Relative to a conventional single-latent mul-timodal VAE, the method introduces a second latent branch, explicit total-correlation regular-ization, and a sparse directional map whose optimization must be scheduled carefully. These additions increase training complexity, especially because total-correlation estimation depends on minibatch-wise interactions and therefore scales less favorably than ordinary reconstruction losses. The method is therefore best positioned as a richer analysis model rather than the lightest possible embedding baseline. That tradeoff is justified only if the resulting representation is biologically more useful. The results here suggest that it is: the same architectural additions that increase optimization complexity are the ones that make branch semantics, sparse regulatory summaries and perturbation-oriented outputs possible [14, 17]. Future work should make this tradeoff more explicit through direct runtime benchmarking and by testing how the current architecture behaves in partially observed or unpaired settings. 

4.5 Limitations and future work 

Several limitations remain. First, the current study is validated mainly on transcriptome-accessibility datasets with matched modalities and does not yet test more general partially observed or unpaired settings, which are already an active target of multimodal translation and benchmarking studies [20, 21, 46]. Second, the causal layer should be interpreted as a structured regulatory hypothesis space, not as definitive proof of causal mechanism. Third, the current ablation study is only complete for PBMC. Extending the same experiments to Brain and E18 would strengthen the generality of the architectural claims. Fourth, the present biological valida-tion is strongest in PBMC, where cell identities and marker sets are relatively well characterized. Additional orthogonal validation would be desirable in more weakly annotated developmental settings. Finally, the same representational logic should be tested in protein-aware settings such 19

## 5 Conclusion 

scDisent introduces a disentangled representation framework with causal structure for single-cell multi-omics. By splitting the latent space into expression and regulatory branches and coupling them through a sparse directed mapping, the model improves both integration performance and biological interpretability. Across PBMC, Human Brain and Mouse E18 [23–25], scDisent achieved the strongest benchmark clustering performance among the tested methods while also producing interpretable perturbation-oriented outputs and quantitative evidence of latent separation. The central claim of the study is not that every inferred edge should be read as established biology, but that the representation itself is more useful for biological reasoning. In conventional entangled models, identity and regulation are mixed together and perturbation-style interpretation becomes structurally ambiguous. In scDisent, the expression branch remains the anchor of cellular identity while the regulatory branch becomes a tractable space for regulator prioritization, lineage-aware interpretation and in silico hypothesis generation. The method therefore narrows the gap between high-performing generative integration and mechanism-aware downstream analysis [14, 17].
