## Abstract 12 

13 

AlphaFold (AF), a deep-learning based protein modelling approach, has revolutionized structural biology 14 

by generally achieving near-experimental accuracy in protein structure prediction. An impactful application 15 

of AF is the modelling of antibodies (Abs) and T-cell receptors (TCRs), key mediators of cellular immunity, 16 

whose structural specificity underlies responses in cancer, infection, and autoimmune diseases. In this work, 17 

we analyse AF3 performance by systematically examining how MSA composition, number of inference 18 

phases and inference parameters affect prediction accuracy and computational efficiency. We present an 19 

acceleration of ~45-fold of the AF3 MSA phase using reduced UniRef90 subsets, combined with up to a 20 

3.6-fold increase in AF3 inference speed through optimized parameters. We provide a highly accurate 21 

variant of the AF3 workflow specifically optimized for the modelling of the Abs and TCRs receptor 22 

domains, enabling rapid, reliable structural predictions at a scale suitable for high-throughput 23 

immunological studies. Our findings provide a foundation for faster therapeutic discovery and deeper 24 

molecular mechanism understanding of immune recognition. 25 

Teaser: By improving key steps in the process, we made AlphaFold3 about 40 times faster at modeling 26 

specific immune proteins 27

## Introduction 28 

29 

The ability to accurately predict protein structures has been revolutionized by AlphaFold (AF)[1], a deep 30 

learning (DL) framework that generally demonstrates near-experimental accuracy across most of the human 31 

proteome. AlphaFold3 (AF3)[2], released in 2024, is the most recent and advanced version of AF for 32 

biomolecular structure prediction and interaction analysis. Unlike AlphaFold2 (AF2)[3], AF3 significantly 33 

reduces the reliance on Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) representations. While MSA information is 34 

still used, its role is greatly diminished in favor of single and pair representations, which helps to simplify 35 

the model’s architecture. The single representation refers to per-residue or per-atom embeddings that 36 

capture local biochemical properties. The pair representation encodes features between every pair of 37 

residues or atoms, capturing spatial proximity, geometric constraints, and co-evolutionary signals. AF3 38 

adopts new components, such as a Pairformer and a Diffusion-based Generative Model, to replace the 39 

Evoformer stack used in AF2. These new modules are responsible for updating both pairwise and single 40 

representations in a more flexible and generalizable manner, allowing AF3 to support heterogeneous 41 

biomolecular assemblies. 42 

Despite the reduced influence of the MSA on model construction in AF3, the MSA component remains the 43 

slowest step in the workflow, representing more than 90% of the end-to-end execution time. Consequently, 44 

MSA generation imposes a disproportionate runtime and emerges as a bottleneck in AF3, notably for 45 

studies requiring the generation of large numbers of 3D structures. Some alternative approaches mitigate 46 

the expensive MSA computation, ColabFold[4] uses a different software to compute the MSA faster while 47 

ImmuneBuilder[5] removes the need of MSA as an input by training their machine learning model 48 

exclusively on immune proteins. 49 

T-cell Receptors (TCRs) and Antibodies (Abs) are key mediators of cellular immunity, whose structural 50 

specificity underlies responses in cancer, infection, and autoimmune diseases. TCRs and Abs exhibit 51 

exceptional sequence diversity and flexible regions, posing challenges in the creation of 3D models. Abs 52

and TCRs are distinct from other proteins due to their conserved structure in the framework region but large 53 

structure variability in the Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDR) regions. Our group develops 3D-54

In this paper, we conducted a comprehensive performance analysis of AF3 for modelling TCRs and Abs. 57 

We focus on two phases of the AF3 workflow: (i) the MSA phase, which prepares evolutionary features 58 

from homologous sequences, and (ii) the inference phase, which performs structure prediction using deep 59 

neural network modules, primarily the Pairformer and Diffusion module, to iteratively update pair 60 

representations that capture atomic interaction relationships. By benchmarking end-to-end execution time 61 

across multiple AF3 workflow variants, with different MSA databases and number of seeds, we 62 

demonstrate that the AF3 efficiency can be improved by using subsets of the UniRef90 database [8] in the 63 

MSA phase ( Figure 1a-b) . On our desktop system, AF3 MSA pipeline execution time is accelerated by up 64 

to 45-fold while keeping the TCRs and Abs models at near-experimental accuracy. We also improved AF3 65 

inference parameters to better fit Ab and TCR modelling, reducing the new bottleneck step of model 66 

generation. By allowing 9 inferences to run in parallel on a single 24Go GPU and optimizing AF3 specific 67 

parameters, we reduced inference time by an additional 1.5 to 3.6-fold depending on input size ( Figure 1a ). 68 

Developing specialized AF3 variants that retain AF’s predictive accuracy while making it extremely fast is 69 

critical for advancing structural immunology and enabling large-scale modeling of immune repertoires. Our 70 

approach could be readily generalized to other protein families with large numbers of experimentally 71 

determined 3D structures in the Protein Data Bank (PDB), such as kinases or proteases or MHC complexes. 72

## Results 82 

83 

In this results section, we present AF3 workflow variants specifically developed for the modelling of the 84 

receptor domain of the Abs and TCRs. We show that the AF3 end-to-end runtime can be substantially 85 

reduced by generating a curated subset of UniRef90 sequences, rather than relying on the full databases 86 

used by AF3. Using the AF3 ranking score, we further demonstrate that a single seed is sufficient to obtain 87 

highly accurate models, eliminating the need for multiple runs. In addition, we accelerate the AF3 inference 88 

stage by up to 3.6-fold through optimized parameter settings. Notably, we show that our AF3 workflow 89 

variant achieves near-experimental accuracy, even for challenging CDRs such as antibody CDRH3 and T-90 

cell receptor CDR3b, while relying solely on the reduced database and a single seed. 91

## Ab modelling 93 

AF uses multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) computed by JackHMMER[9] to extract information from 94 

evolutionarily related sequences corresponding to the input proteins. Homologous sequences are retrieved 95 

for each protein chain in a very time-consuming process, reading sequences from huge databases such as 96 

UniRef90, which contains 153,742,194 sequences, to ensure general applicability. 97 

To evaluate the Uniref90 sequences selected by AF3 for the modelling of TCRs we used a non-redundant 98 

set of 3’213 TCRs from the VDJ database [10, 11] (Methods ). By sequentially generating MSAs for 99

database after treating 500 TCRs (15% of the number of samples used) already contains more than 95% of 101 

the total number of sequences, becoming sufficient to reproduce the UniRef90-based MSAs of all 102 

subsequent TCRs ( Figure 2 a-c ). Across the entire VDJ set, 261,055 sequences are used for TCRα ( Figure 103 

2a and 2g ), 208,047 for TCRβ ( Figure 2b and 2g) , and 261,780 for TCRαβ ( Figure 2 c) and 2g ), indicating 104 

that the α chain incorporates more UniRef90 sequences than the β chain, with substantial overlap between 105

## keeping accuracy near experimental resolution 158 

To evaluate the impact of reducing the reference database used for MSA, we assessed model performance 159 

and accuracy across different AF3 workflow variants that incorporate sequence databases of varying sizes. 160 

Model accuracy was quantified by calculating the root mean square deviation (RMSD) between the 161 

predicted 3D structure generated with a given AF3 variant (both the original and the reduced-database 162 

versions) and the corresponding experimental PDB structure. Figure 3. 163 

Our systems of interest, TCRs and Abs, were selected because of their structural similarities, and the well-164 

known challenges associated with accurately modeling their loops[5, 12, 13]. TCRs and Abs share a 165 

broadly similar 3D architecture ( Figure 3 ): both are heterodimers containing six complementarity-166 

determining regions (CDRs) that form their binding interfaces. As illustrated in Figure 3 , CDRH1, CDRH2, 167 

and CDRH3 from the Ab heavy chain (H) correspond to CDRβ1, CDRβ2, and CDRβ3 of the TCR β chain; 168 

likewise, the three CDRs of the Ab light chain (L) align with those of the TCR α chain. Despite these 169 

structural parallels, Abs can bind to an extraordinarily wide range of molecular targets, including peptides, 170 

proteins, and haptens, whereas αβ TCRs recognize peptide antigens presented by MHC molecules. 171

## AF3 parameters optimization during inference allows for substantial speed up for 350 

## modelling small proteins 351 

First, by reducing the default GPU memory pre-allocation to 10% instead of 95% we were able to run up 352 

to 9 inferences in parallel on a single 24Gb GPU. While these numbers are specific to the hardware used 353 

(10% allowed us to generate models of up to 312 tokens), we hypothesize that on a GPU with more 354 

dedicated memory we could run even more inferences in parallel. Parallelizing inferences allows to reduce 355 

the featurization and the first seed inference time, respectively from 7 to 1 second and from 36 to 23 seconds 356 

(Figure 10a ), but not subsequent seeds. This probably means that only the model compilation and 357 

featurization which are handled by the CPU were accelerated through this parallelization. 358 

To further reduce inference time, we tailored bucket size to the exact length of our inputs (1 amino acid = 359 

1 token) and observed a speed up proportional to the amount of padding tokens eliminated ( Figure 10 b ). 360 

The most striking example was a 272 tokens Ab that would by default use a bucket of 512 tokens resulting 361 

in 240 padding tokens. The inference of the first and second seeds respectively took 65.5 and 50.5 seconds 362 

with default bucket while it only took 34 and 17.3 seconds with optimized bucket size, this showed a 363 

roughly flat 30 seconds time gain per seed inference. This optimization is not hardware specific and would 364 

provide significant speed up to any processus needing to generate large number of small models with AF3. 365 

We did not analyse more seeds because all subsequent seeds after the second one are identical to it 366 

(Supporting Information Figure 8 ). 367

Despite the reduced influence of the MSA on model construction in AF3, the MSA component remains the 373 

slowest step in the workflow, representing more than 90% of the end-to-end execution time. Consequently, 374 

MSA generation imposes a disproportionate runtime and emerges as a bottleneck in AF3. 375 

To largely decrease the end to end execution time of AF3 to generate structural models of TCR or Ab 376 

receptor domains, four different AF3 MSA variants were developed: no MSA, the original AF3 setup with 377 

the full UniRef90 database, a UniRef90 subset selected according to AF3’s choices during TCR and Ab 378

All the models obtained with the AF3 MSA-free variant are significantly worse than the ones obtained with 381

far fewer TCR experimental 3D structures were available for AF3 training compared to antibodies. 387 

Although some TCRs are better modelled than others in the MSA-free variant, we do not observe that the 388

Despite using highly size-reduced subsets of UniRef90 for MSA, all our accelerated Af3 workflows lead 391 

to results comparable to the native AF3 workflow. The RMSD differences are comparable indicating that 392 

reduced-database MSAs can effectively compete with the full AF3 reference database for some conserved 393

## Custom Uniref90-derived databases creation: 452 

Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) of the TCR sequences was performed using JackHMMER [9] 453 

(EVALUE=1e-3, NUM_ITER=3) against the UniRef90 database[8, 17]. All unique aligned sequences were 454 

aggregated into a new database called UniRef-TCR composed of 261’780 sequences, among which 455 

261’055 were aligned with the alpha chain and 208’047 with the beta chain. The EVALUE threshold of 1e-456 

3 used here is less stringent than the 1e-4 EVALUE typically used in AF3 MSA runs. This relaxed criterion 457 

allows for faster sequence expansion and the identification of more distant homologs. The goal is to avoid 458 

overly constraining the sequence space, providing AF with a reduced set but still with a broader diversity 459 

of sequences. 460 

Another subset of UniRef90 was created by searching in the database for the words "t-cell receptor", "t cell 461 

receptor", "Tcell receptor", "immunoglobulin", "antibody", "nanobody" in the sequences header. This 462 

resulted in a non-exhaustive set of sequences with a total of 41’055 sequences gathered this way. We called 463 

this new database UniRef-textmining. 464

single Complementary Determining Region’s (CDR) residues, on a set of all CDRs residues or on all 529 

residues of the model. To determine CDRs and framework residues the ANARCI software was used[16]. 530 

For TCRs, the IMGT numbering scheme was used while the Chothia numbering scheme was preferred for 531 

antibodies. 532

seeds, the highest score among the 20 inferences). RMSD of all CDRs residues were then computed. For 536 

Abs, only 10 seeds were generated. 537

## AF3 inference optimization (GPU optimization): 546 

Bucket optimization comparison was performed with the --buckets argument set to the exact same number 547 

of tokens as the input or left to the default bucket sizes (256 or 512 tokens). Parallelization was performed 548 

with optimized buckets, by creating an AlphaFold docker image with 10% pre-allocated memory 549 

(Dockerfile : ENV XLA_CLIENT_MEM_FRACTION=0.10) then inferences were launched in parallel. 550 

To assess the overall speed up a set of 100 random Abs from the validation set were modelled with 551 

optimized parameters. Featurization and seed inference times were retrieved through the logs of AF3. Note 552 

that computing 9 inferences in parallel necessitated about 40Go of RAM usage. 553
