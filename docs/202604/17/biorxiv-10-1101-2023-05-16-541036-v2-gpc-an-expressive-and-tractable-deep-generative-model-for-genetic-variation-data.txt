Abstract 13 

Generative models play an important role in population genetics, being used to generate artificial 14 

genomes (AGs) that help benchmark methods, test evolutionary hypotheses, and construct reference 15 

panels for imputation while working around data-sharing restrictions. Existing generative models of ge-16 

netic variation, however, struggle to faithfully express dependencies in the data while retaining tractability 17 

and preserving privacy. 18 

We introduce Genetic Probabilistic Circuits ( GPC ), a deep generative model for genetic variation 19 

data based on hidden Chow-Liu trees represented as probabilistic circuits. GPC generalizes traditional 20 

hidden Markov models by allowing arbitrary tree structures over latent variables, enabling it to capture 21 

long-range dependencies among SNPs. GPC is tractable, supporting exact computation of marginal and 22 

conditional probabilities that enables both AG generation and direct genotype imputation (avoiding the 23 

need for simulating AGs). 24 

We show that GPC generates AGs that accurately reproduce population structure and linkage dise-25 

quilibrium patterns across a range of length scales. Compared to other deep generative approaches, GPC 26 

consistently improves imputation accuracy, with particularly strong gains for low-frequency variants and 27 

in populations that are not well-represented in public reference panels. Finally, we show that GPC 28 

better preserves privacy of the training data, thereby providing a practical framework for AG generation 29 

in settings with limited data access. 30

Classical population genetic simulators rely on the coalescent model [11], which generates genomes based 40 

on demographic history together with mutation and recombination rates. The coalescent with recombina-41 

tion [12] connects observed genetic variation to latent genealogies that change along the genome [13] and is 42 

highly expressive, but exact inference under this model is computationally challenging, motivating tractable 43 

approximations. One class approximates the coalescent as a Markovian process along the genome [14, 15], 44 

forming the basis of widely used simulators such as msprime [9] and its extensions [10]. A second class 45 

dispenses with explicit genealogical modeling and instead directly approximates the data distribution. A46 

prominent example is the product-of-approximate-conditionals (PAC) model [16], which naturally yields 47 

a hidden Markov model (HMM) [17]. HMM-based models have proven highly successful in applications 48 

including haplotype phasing [18, 19], genotype imputation [20, 3], and ancestry inference [21, 22]. 49 

Motivated by the success of deep learning, recent work has explored deep generative models as an al-50 

ternative approach to represent genetic variation [23, 24, 25]. Models based on generative adversarial net-51 

works (GANs) [25, 26, 27, 28], variational autoencoders (VAEs) [23, 29, 30], restricted Boltzmann machines 52 

(RBMs) [25], and more recently di !usion models [31] can be more expressive than HMMs and often pro-53 

duce AGs that visually resemble real genomes in principal component analysis (PCA) or allele frequency 54 

summaries. However, these models have critical limitations for genomic applications. First, GANs do not 55 

define a probability distribution over the data, making likelihood-based inference impossible. RBMs define 56 

a distribution but require computing an intractable partition function over exponentially many configu-57 

rations, preventing exact likelihood evaluation. VAEs define a probability distribution but the marginal 58 

likelihood requires integrating over a continuous latent space with a nonlinear decoder, which is intractable; 59 

only a lower bound (the ELBO) is accessible in practice. Di !usion models can in principle compute exact 60 

likelihoods via probability flow ordinary di !erential equations, but scale poorly to SNP data: existing ap-61 

proaches [31] require lossy dimensionality reduction (e.g., per-gene PCA) as a preprocessing step before the 62 

model operates [31], and have not been evaluated on imputation tasks. Second, none of these approaches 63 

support e ”cient conditional probability estimation. This limitation makes model comparison di ”cult and 64 

limits their use in downstream tasks such as genotype imputation, which require computing the probability 65 

of unobserved variants conditioned on observed variants. A common workaround involves generating AGs 66 

from these models, which then serve as a reference panel for imputation methods such as Impute5, Beagle, 67 

or Minimac [32, 33, 34], though this approach can introduce additional noise in the imputation process. 68 

Third, training these models is challenging due to complex optimization objectives and large hyperparam-69 

eter spaces, and convergence must be assessed through subjective visual inspection rather than principled 70 

likelihood monitoring. These di ”culties collectively highlight the need for deep generative models that are 71 

both expressive and tractable. 72 

We address this need by introducing GPC (Genetic Probabilistic Circuits), a class of deep generative 73 

models that fits genetic variation data with high accuracy while supporting e ”cient and exact inference. To 74 

model the distribution over single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in an individual, we propose a latent 75 

variable model in which each SNP is associated with a hidden variable and the hidden variables form a 76

# 2 Results 103 

## 2.1 Methods Overview 104 

We present GPC , a deep generative model for genetic variation data. GPC is based on hidden Chow-105 

Liu trees (HCLTs) [35], a class of latent variable models that generalize hidden Markov models (HMMs), 106 

represented as probabilistic circuits (PCs) [37, 38] for e ”cient inference and learning. 107 

Hidden Chow-Liu Trees HCLTs are tree-structured graphical models that represent the probability 108 

distribution over observed random variables, X = ( X1, . . . , X N ). In the context of genetic variation across 109 

N SNPs, each observed Xn represents the genotype at SNP n (e.g., Xn → {0, 1} for haploid genomes). 110 

HCLTs rely on the following key designs: 111 

• Hidden Variable Mapping: For every observed variable Xn, the model introduces a corresponding 112 

hidden variable Zn (which takes one of L discrete values). Together, these form hidden variables 113 

Z = ( Z1, . . . , Z N ). 114

• Chow-Liu Tree Architecture: Each observed variable Xn connects solely to its hidden counterpart Zn.115 

The hidden variables, however, connect to each other to form a tree structure. This tree is learned 116 

using the Chow-Liu algorithm [39] to capture the strongest pairwise correlations between variables 117 

(Figure 1). 118 

• Parameterization: The entire HCLT is defined by just two sets of conditional probability distributions: 119 

the emissions P (Xn|Zn) and the tree transitions P (Zn|ZPa( n)), where Pa( n) denotes the parent of node 120 

n in the hidden variable tree. 121 

The critical distinction from HMMs is structural. Classical HMMs impose a chain structure where hidden 122 

variables corresponding to consecutive SNPs are adjacent in the tree ( Z1 ↑ Z2 ↑ . . . ↑ ZN ). This chain 123 

topology can only propagate information between distant SNPs through all intermediate variables. HCLTs 124 

relax this constraint by allowing arbitrary tree structures, so that SNPs with strong long-range correlations 125 

(such as X1 and X5 being highly correlated without being correlated with intermediate SNPs) can be placed 126 

close together in the tree (Figure 1). This flexible structure allows HCLTs to more faithfully capture linkage 127 

disequilibrium patterns at all length scales. 128 

Probabilistic Circuits and Tractable Inference While HCLTs are more expressive than HMMs, 129 

naively training and performing inference in HCLTs at the scale required for modern genomic datasets would 130 

be computationally prohibitive. Our key insight is to represent HCLTs as probabilistic circuits (PCs) [37, 38]. 131 

A PC represents a joint probability distribution through a directed acyclic graph (DAG) consisting of input, 132 

sum, and product nodes. Product nodes define factorized distributions over their inputs, while sum nodes 133 

represent mixtures weighted by learnable parameters. Under structural constraints known as smoothness 134 

and decomposability, arbitrary marginal queries can be computed in time linear in the size of the circuit. 135 

This tractability is what enables GPC to perform three key operations e ”ciently: (1) computing likelihoods 136 

on held-out data for principled model comparison, (2) generating artificial genomes via ancestral sampling, 137 

and (3) performing direct genotype imputation through exact conditional probability computation. By rep-138 

resenting HCLTs as equivalent PCs and training them using the PC package PyJuice [40], we can extensively 139 

parallelize Expectation-Maximization (EM) updates on GPUs, enabling e ”cient training even for models 140 

with over 88 million parameters (Section 4.2). 141 

Generating AGs from GPC is performed via ancestral sampling through the circuit in time linear in 142 

the circuit size. For genotype imputation, GPC computes conditional probabilities p(Xmissing |Xobserved )143 

directly as ratios of marginal queries, both of which can be evaluated via a single feedforward pass through 144 

the circuit. This direct imputation capability, unique to GPC among the deep generative approaches we 145 

consider, eliminates the need for intermediate AG generation and, as we show, yields consistently higher 146 

accuracy (see Section 4.2.2 for full details on sampling and conditional inference). 147

## 2.3 Evaluation 154 

Baselines We compare GPC against two categories of baselines. The first category consists of deep 155 

generative models that have been previously proposed for AG generation: (1) generative adversarial networks 156 

(WGAN ) and (2) restricted Boltzmann machines ( RBM ) as implemented in [25]. We focus our comparison 157 

on these two methods as they are the most established deep generative approaches specifically designed and 158 

benchmarked for the local genetic structure and imputation tasks we evaluate here. More recent approaches 159 

including VAEs [29, 30] and di !usion models [31] have been proposed for related but distinct tasks, and do 160 

not support e ”cient, tractable probabilistic inference; we leave systematic comparison with these methods 161 

to future work. For both WGAN and RBM baselines, we use the samples generated by the corresponding 162 

authors for comparison 1 in our 1KG experiments, but we also retrain their models on our own training split 163 

to ensure a fair comparison. In imputation experiments, we additionally compare against Impute5 [32], a 164 

state-of-the-art imputation tool widely used in genetics. Impute5 serves a dual role: it is used with real 165 

reference genomes to establish performance benchmarks, and it is used with AG reference panels generated 166 

by each method to evaluate their utility for imputation. 167 

The second category consists of simpler probabilistic graphical models (PGMs) that support tractable 168 

likelihood computation: fully-factorized distributions ( Indep ), Markov chain models of order 1 ( Markov ), 169 

and non-homogeneous hidden Markov models ( HMM ). These serve as baselines for density estimation and 170 

AG quality comparisons. 171 

Evaluation criteria We evaluate these models using the following metrics: (1) log-likelihood on test data 172 

to assess the capability of each model as a density estimator; (2) summaries of AGs sampled from each 173 

model, including the top principal components and linkage disequilibrium at pairs of SNPs; (3) genotype 174 

imputation, which evaluates the models’ utility on an important downstream task; and (4) privacy analysis 175 

of the AGs sampled from each model. 176 

## 2.4 GPC trains e !ciently with objective convergence criteria 177 

Training GPC requires minimal hyperparameter tuning. We set the number of latent states to 128 (the 178 

maximum feasible given memory constraints) and use a small pseudocount (0.005) for smoothing. Models 179 

were trained for 2,000–5,000 epochs depending on dataset size. We use the PC package PyJuice [40] to train 180 

GPC e”ciently on GPUs, achieving EM updates in under 2, 10, and 3 seconds per epoch on the 1KG, 181 

UKBB, and high-coverage 1KG datasets, respectively. On a single NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPU with 24GB 182 

available memory, GPC trains in roughly 2, 6, and 4 hours on each dataset, respectively. Once the model is 183 

loaded, generating a single sample can be done in under 2, 5, and 3 seconds for each dataset, respectively. 184 

Imputing all missing SNPs within a single sample can be done in 20 milliseconds. A key advantage over 185 

other deep generative approaches is that convergence can be monitored via held-out log-likelihood, providing 186 

an objective stopping criterion rather than subjective visual inspection. Full training details for GPC and 187 

all baseline methods are provided in Section 4.4. 188

## 2.6 GPC improves imputation accuracy across diverse scenarios 224 

A key downstream application of generative models and AGs is genotype imputation. To assess the quality 225 

of artificial genomes (AGs) produced by GPC and competing generative models, we evaluate their utility in 226 

several imputation settings across the datasets described in Section 2.2. One approach is to generate AGs 227 

from these models, which then serve as reference panels for Impute5 [32] and other standard imputation 228 

tools. Alternatively, as described in Section 2.1, GPC can perform direct imputation by leveraging e ”cient 229 

conditional probability computation over the learned model, without the need to generate AGs. 230 

Following the same training protocol as in Section 2.5, we use 80/20 train/test splits and generate the 231 

same number of AGs per dataset. For population-specific experiments, the same 80/20 split is applied 232 

within the target population. Following the experimental protocol established in prior work [32, 33, 44], we 233

in access. We consider two sets of target populations: all individuals of non-European ancestry and all 257 

individuals of African ancestry. In each of these settings, we also explore how the accuracy of imputation is 258

2.6.2 Population-Specific Imputation 280 

We next consider imputation in populations for which reference genomes are limited or restricted. Because 281 

large public datasets are predominantly of European ancestry, we consider scenarios where the target popula-282 

tion consists of individuals of either non-European or African ancestry. In this setting, imputation accuracy 283 

in the target population can be degraded when public European reference genomes are the only data avail-284 

able to the imputation method. AGs specific to the target population generated by GPC can mitigate this 285 

challenge by learning from an ancestry-matched set of private reference genomes. We also evaluate combined 286 

reference panels formed by augmenting real European data with population-specific AGs. 287 

Analogous to the general imputation experiments, GPC (direct) remains the most accurate across MAF 288 

bins (Figures 6 and S6 for 1KG and UKBB respectively). Averaged across datasets and ancestries, GPC 289 

(direct) achieves a 0.154 (33%) improvement in r2 over the next best method, RBM (0.202 (279%) for 290

the distributional mismatch between the reference and target populations. This holds consistently across 293 

the 1KG dataset. In the UKBB dataset, GPC (direct) outperforms Impute5 for common variants but is 294 

slightly less accurate for rare variants. We attribute this to our balanced sampling strategy (Section 4.1.2), 295 

in which the European reference panel available to Impute5 and the population-specific training data for 296 

the generative models are matched in sample size. Under this design, a large European reference panel 297 

(even one mismatched in ancestry) can partially compensate for distributional mismatch at rare variants, 298 

where population-specific sample sizes are most limiting. Importantly, when the real European genomes are 299 

incorporated into the training of the generative models (the “combined” setting), all methods improve, and 300 

GPC (direct combined) remains competitive with or above Impute5 across MAF bins, indicating that the 301 

gains from ancestry matching and from larger sample size are complementary. Averaged across datasets 302 

and ancestries, GPC (direct) achieves a 0.056 (12.3%) improvement in r2 over Impute5 using European 303 

genomes (0.012 (42.1%) for low-frequency variants). Finally, we find that combining population-specific 304 

AGs with European genomes consistently improves accuracy across all MAF bins. Averaged across datasets 305 

and ancestries, GPC (direct) achieves a 0.011 (1.8%) improvement in r2 over itself when including European 306 

genomes in the reference panel (0.023 (12.3%) for low-frequency variants). 307

the sample size of the private population-specific data, and the frequency of the variants being imputed. 312

in our balanced UKBB design, gains over a large European-only panel are most pronounced for common 314

## 2.7 GPC balances considerations of privacy and utility 335 

To evaluate the privacy of the AGs, we compute the nearest neighbor adversarial accuracy ( AAT S ) metric 336 

introduced by Yale et al. [45]. The metric decomposes into two components: AA TRUTH , which measures 337 

whether real samples are closer to other real samples than to synthetic ones, and AA SYN , which measures 338 

the analogous property for synthetic samples. Values near 0.5 for both components indicate an ideal balance 339 

between utility and privacy. We report AA TRUTH and AA SYN separately rather than their average, as the 340 

overall AAT S can mask pathological cases where poor values in one component cancel those in the other. 341 

We evaluate on both training and held-out test sets and standardize genotypes prior to distance computation 342 

as this upweights the impact of rare variants which tend to be more challenging to model (see Section 4.5.1 343 

for details). 344 

Under this evaluation, GPC achieves AA SYN closest to 0.5 across all splits and both datasets, and 345 

AA TRUTH closest to 0.5 in all but one case (1KG test, where WGAN is marginally closer: 0.818 vs. 0.820), 346 

indicating the best overall balance between utility and privacy (Table 2). The near-zero AA SYN values for 347 

RBM paired with near-unity AA TRUTH reveal a distinct failure mode: real samples still find other real sam-348 

ples as their nearest neighbors (real-to-real distances are smaller than real-to-synthetic), but each synthetic 349 

sample is closer to some individual real genome than to any other synthetic sample. Rather than form-350 

ing a coherent distribution, RBM produces samples that float near individual training haplotypes without 351 

capturing broader structure, a privacy risk because each synthetic genome can potentially be traced back 352 

to a specific training individual. Conversely, WGAN exhibits uniformly high values for both components, 353 

indicating that its synthetic distribution occupies a region largely disjoint from the real data, sacrificing 354 

utility. We note that even for GPC , AA TRUTH remains noticeably above 0.5, suggesting that the AGs are 355 

still somewhat distinguishable from real data. 356

# 3 Discussion 357 

In this work, we introduced GPC , a deep generative model for genetic variation based on hidden Chow-Liu 358 

trees (HCLTs) and probabilistic circuits (PCs). Unlike prior deep generative models (e.g., GANs, VAEs, and 359 

RBMs), GPC combines tractable probabilistic inference with an expressive latent variable structure, enabling 360 

principled convergence monitoring via held-out log-likelihood, accurate reconstruction of population structure 361 

and linkage disequilibrium patterns across all distance scales, and direct genotype imputation through exact 362 

conditional probability computation. In population-specific settings, GPC (direct) consistently outperforms 363 

other deep generative methods as well as Impute5 using publicly available European reference genomes, 364 

with particularly strong gains for low-frequency variants that are disproportionately population-specific. We 365 

also found that GPC AGs provide improved privacy properties compared to RBMs and WGANs, though 366 

we emphasize that demonstrating improved performance on a single metric does not guarantee complete 367 

privacy preservation. Other attack models, such as membership inference attacks [46, 47] or attribute 368 

inference attacks [48], may still pose risks. Future work should explore whether GPC can be extended to 369 

provide formal privacy guarantees through di !erentially private EM algorithms [49]. 370 

Despite these advantages, several limitations remain. First, scaling GPC to full genome length remains 371 

challenging and will likely require hierarchical or distributed approaches. A straightforward approach to 372 

whole genome data would involve applying GPC to LD blocks which are treated as independentof each 373 

other though such an approach is likely to ignore LD across blocks. Second, our current implementation of 374 

GPC is restricted to haploid genomes to enable direct comparisons with prior approaches. Extending GPC 375 

to generate diploid genotype data would increase the applicability of the model. Third, like all generative 376 

approaches, GPC inherits biases from the training data, and ensuring fairness across diverse cohorts remains 377 

an open challenge. Fourth, additional downstream benchmarks such as rare variant association studies, fine-378 

mapping, or polygenic risk scores would provide a more complete picture of utility. Finally, our experiments 379 

focused on single genomic regions; genome-wide evaluation would better assess robustness across varying LD 380 

structures and recombination rates. 381 

Additionally, recent work has extended the space of deep generative approaches for genetic data to include 382 

VAEs [29, 30], GANs operating in reduced-dimensional spaces [26, 28], and di !usion models [31], as well as 383 

genome-wide WGAN and CRBM variants [25]. Systematically comparing GPC against these approaches 384 

across imputation and other downstream tasks, and extending all methods to genome-wide scales, remains 385 

an important direction for future work. 386

4.2.2 Probabilistic Circuits 450

## 4.4 Training Specifications 534 

Training GPC is straightforward, requiring minimal hyperparameter tuning. We set the number of latent 535 

states (for each hidden variable in the HCLT) to 128 for both datasets – the maximum feasible given memory 536 

constraints. We observe that changing the pseudocount parameter (used to smooth probability estimates) 537 

does not significantly impact the training procedure and can be left at a small default value (0.005). 538 

Based on initial experiments with small validation sets, we find that training for 2,000–5,000 epochs 539 

works well for these datasets, though longer training is possible without overfitting (with minimal perfor-540 

mance gains). For the 1KG data, we trained GPC for 5,000 epochs. Due to the larger sample size (and 541 

longer training time per epoch) in the UKBB data, we stopped at 2,000 epochs. Although we do not tune 542 

the pseudocount, this would be the only parameter to consider adjusting for further optimization. Addi-543 

tionally, since we use full-batch EM to learn the circuit parameters, we do not need to tune other standard 544 

hyperparameters such as learning rate or batch size. 545 

A key advantage of GPC over other deep generative approaches is that we can probabilistically deter-546 

mine convergence by monitoring held-out log-likelihood, which provides an objective, quantitative stopping 547 

criterion. This stands in contrast to methods that lack tractable likelihoods, where convergence must be 548 

assessed through slower, less consistent visual inspection methods that are more prone to human error. 549 

The baseline RBM and WGAN methods require substantially more time and experimentation to tune, 550 

primarily due to their larger hyperparameter spaces and inability to calculate likelihoods or determine 551 

convergence probabilistically. For RBMs, the partition function is intractable due to an exponentially large 552 

number of configurations over the visible and hidden nodes. Therefore, we must rely on indirect metrics such 553 

as Nearest Neighbor Adversarial Accuracy (AATS) [45] and visual overlap in principal component space. 554 

Similarly, WGANs do not define a probability distribution over the data, preventing direct evaluation of 555 

sample likelihoods. Here too, we must rely on visual overlap in the principal component space to assess 556 

convergence, a subjective and time-consuming process. 557 

We utilize the training code and notebooks provided by the authors on GitHub to tune both methods. 558 

Although we initially struggled to achieve optimal performance on the 1KG African data subset, we resolved 559 

this issue after consulting with the authors. For the RBM, we modified several parameters (e.g., higher 560 

learning rate, fewer epochs, fewer Gibbs sampling steps). For the WGAN, we increased the batch size. 561 

Otherwise, all other parameters were kept at their default values. When training on the UKBB data, we 562 

retained the default parameters; similar to the GPC case, we used fewer epochs due to longer training time 563 

but still achieved good overlap in the principal component space based on visual inspection. 564 

The simpler PGM baselines, Indep and Markov , model the observed SNPs directly without latent 565 

variables. Indep treats each SNP as an independent Bernoulli random variable, estimating P (Xn = 1) 566 

separately for each site via its sample mean (calculated by a single pass over the training data). Markov 567 

is a first-order Markov chain: it models each SNP conditioned only on the immediately preceding one in 568 

genomic order, P (Xn | Xn→1), and is likewise fit in closed form via maximum likelihood. Both models 569 

therefore require no iterative training. For HMM , we use the EM algorithm with random initialization. 570 

Similar to GPC , we set the number of latent states for HMM at 128 and train for 2,000–5,000 epochs 571 

depending on the dataset. Using these settings, the held-out log-likelihood converged e !ectively. We tried 572 

higher values of latent states for HMM but these all resulted in worse log-likelihoods and slower training 573 

overall. 574
