Abstract 15 

Preclinical antibody discovery relies on progressive screening and down-selection of candidate 16 

antibodies from large immune repertoires, yet this critical process is poorly represented in existing 17 

public databases. Here we introduce KyDab (Kymouse Antibody Database), a well-curated database 18 

of antibody discovery selection data generated using standardized workflows on the Kymouse 19 

humanized mouse platform. The current release includes 11 Kymouse platform mice immunisation 20 

studies covering 51 immunogens, more than 120,000 paired heavy–light chain sequences, and binding 21 

measurements for a selected subset of experimentally characterized clones. By capturing full-funnel 22 

selection data with consistent metadata and both positive and negative experimental outcomes, 23 

KyDab provides a valuable data resource for the development and evaluation of artificial intelligence 24 

models for antibody discovery. KyDab is accessible at https://kydab.naturalantibody.com/, and the 25 

database will be continuously updated as new datasets become available. 26

Monoclonal antibodies have emerged as the fastest-growing class of biopharmaceuticals, with over 32 

150 antibodies approved by the FDA to date 1 . Due to their superior specificity, potency, and safety 33 

profiles, antibody-based therapeutics are widely regarded as leading candidates for treating cancer, 34 

immune-mediated disorders, and infectious diseases 2. In recent years, advances in artificial 35 

intelligence have begun to reshape antibody drug discovery workflows. Approaches such as virtual 36 

screening, affinity maturation, and generative design are increasingly integrated into early discovery, 37 

offering opportunities to accelerate development timelines and reduce costs 3. These methods, 38 

however, rely heavily on large, high-quality datasets with experimentally validated outcomes to 39 

enable effective model training and benchmarking 4 .40 

41 

To support the growing data needs of AI development, several public resources curate large antibody 42 

sequence datasets (e.g., Observed Antibody Space; 5 ) and structural annotations (e.g., SAbDab; 6 ). 43 

While these databases provide broad coverage, they do not capture the real-world process of 44 

therapeutic antibody discovery as practiced in an industrial setting. Modern discovery pipelines, 45 

including phage display, yeast display and animal immunisation, typically generate large pools of 46 

clones, followed by multiple stages of down-selection and affinity characterisation using assays such 47 

as surface plasmon resonance (SPR). Existing specialised therapeutic antibody databases, such as 48 

Thera-SAbDab 7 and IMGT/mAb-KG 8 , focus primarily on the relatively small set of mature, 49 

clinically advanced antibodies. To our knowledge, no public resource currently provides full-funnel 50 

datasets that reflect the practical selection and screening steps used in real-world antibody discovery 51 

workflows. 52 

53 

Beyond these limitations in dataset representation, another critical gap concerns the scarcity and bias 54 

of affinity-characterized antibodies 9 . This is largely due to several factors - manual curation is labor-55 

intensive, affinity values are often presented only as figures or partial tables, and negative screening 56 

data are rarely reported. Even in newer structural resources such as SAAINT-DB, which reviewed 57

Broader protein interaction databases, including SKEMPI v2.0 11 , AbBind 12 , Affinity Benchmark 59

65 

To begin addressing these challenges, we present KyDab (Kymouse Antibody Database), a 66 

comprehensive database of antibody discovery selection datasets. KyDab provides systematically 67 

curated antibody sequences with comprehensive metadata and, where available, experimentally 68 

measured binding data. All datasets originate from Kymouse 15 , a state-of-the-art humanized mouse 69 

platform used for therapeutic antibody discovery. Consistent single-cell processing and standardized 70 

data analysis pipelines are applied to minimise technical variability and ensure comparability across 71 

campaigns. The current release spans 11 immunisation studies, comprising 51 unique immunogens, 72 

and 123,527 productive heavy–light chain pairs, with 1,657 clones associated with binding data. By 73 

making such campaign data available, KyDab establishes a resource that acts as a real-world 74 

benchmark for general-purpose antibody predictors. As such we expect that the database will foster 75 

development of more robust algorithms that can be applied in production in therapeutic programs. 76

Results 108 

Overview of the database 109 

110 

All datasets deposited in the portal are generated from mice derived from the Kymouse platform, a 111 

transgenic mouse platform developed by Kymab for discovery of therapeutic human monoclonal 112 

antibodies. These mice are genetically engineered to produce antibodies with variable heavy and light 113 

chain regions (VH and VL) of entirely human origin. Over the past decade, this humanized mouse 114 

platform has proven to be robust, stable, and versatile for therapeutic antibody discovery, with several 115 

antibodies derived from this platform having advanced to clinical trials 18 19 .116 

117 

KyDab comprises 11 human antibody studies encompassing 51 unique immunogens generated from 118 

the Kymouse platform, targeting a diverse array of viral, bacterial, and parasitic antigens 15 16 . In 119 

general, immunogens were either selected from well-established commercial antigen products or 120 

designed by structural biologists with expertise in pathogen-specific immunogenic structures. 121 

Following immunisation with the respective immunogens, sera were collected and assessed for 122 

reactivity using screening assays such as ELISA to evaluate immune responses. Mice demonstrating 123 

positive immune responses were selected for further processing. 124 

125 

From each selected mouse, spleen, lymph node, and/or bone marrow cells were harvested and 126 

subjected to flow cytometry-based single-cell sorting to isolate antigen-specific B cells for 127 

downstream sequencing. The resulting paired VH-VL sequences underwent analysis and down-128

selection through an in-house bioinformatics pipeline that is similar to the Immcantation 129 

framework 20 . Sequences were clustered into lineages based on inferred V and J gene usage and 130 

CDR3 sequence identity, with representative clones from the more expanded lineages preferentially 131 

selected. Additional selection criteria included somatic hypermutation frequency, evidence of 132 

sequence convergence, developability assessments, and overall sequence diversity. The scale of clone 133 

selection varied across studies according to specific research objectives. In most studies, selected 134 

clones were recombinantly expressed and further characterized through in vitro affinity assays to 135 

assess binding to their corresponding antigens, as illustrated in Figure 1 . The portal provides 136 

comprehensive data including all paired antibody sequences, germline gene annotations, complete 137 

metadata (mouse ID, tissue source, immunogen identity), and where available, affinity measurements 138 

and antigen sequences in FASTA format. To ensure uniform representation of the data, we post-139 

processed raw read files using RIOT 17 , a sequence annotation tool that translates nucleotide 140 

sequences into amino acids and aligns them to the IMGT scheme. 141 

142 

For friendly user experience, KyDab also features simple structured summaries, interactive 143 

visualisations, and flexible download options, enabling researchers to access and apply these data 144 

with minimal need for additional engineering or background reading. 145

Repertoire diversity across datasets 157 

To evaluate the diversity of the antibody repertoires, we performed sequence identity clustering on the 158 

heavy and light chain variable regions with respect to IMGT variable region alignment. The raw 159 

number of sequences and the resulting cluster counts for each dataset, chain type, and region are 160 

detailed in Table 2 . This analysis focused on the complementarity-determining regions ( CDR1, 161 

CDR2, CDR3 ) and the framework regions ( FW ) using sequence identity thresholds of 90%, 80%, 162

of the clustering strictness is also evident when comparing the panels of Figure 2 from top to bottom; 174

analysis highlights the varied complexity of antibody repertoires and confirms that our collection 177 

encompasses a broad range of immune responses suitable for robust immunological investigation. 178

Discussion 194 

195 

Despite the rapid growth of public antibody databases, there is currently no centralized resource that 196 

captures antibody discovery data to such a large scale. Most existing public resources of therapeutic 197 

antibody primarily curate clinically successful antibodies from sparse origins, representing only a 198 

small fraction of the total discovery funnel and seldom include negative or low-binding clones 7 21 .199 

200 

KyDab addresses this gap by providing a comprehensive antibody discovery dataset generated under 201 

high quality standard and consistent workflows. All data were produced using a well-established 202 

humanized mouse platform with standardized immunisation, single-cell sequencing, selection and 203 

expression pipelines across multiple studies. This level of upstream consistency is rarely achieved in 204 

public antibody resources. Importantly, the dataset reflects the full process of antibody down-205 

selection, from large repertoires of paired VH–VL sequences to a defined high-potential subset 206 

advanced to experimental testing. Such data are particularly valuable for AI applications aimed at 207 

improving the success rate of experimental validation. In addition, the antigen diversity in KyDab also 208 

provides a more challenging and realistic benchmark for evaluating model robustness across targets. 209 

210 

As with all antibody discovery workflows, the dataset reflects biases introduced by bioinformatics-211 

guided down-selection. Sequences advanced to expression are enriched for expanded lineages, higher 212 

somatic mutation levels and predicted developability, leading to an overrepresentation of true binders 213 

and high-affinity antibodies. Nevertheless, KyDab includes both positive and negative experimental 214 

outcomes. The availability of negative data is critical for proper model calibration and for evaluating 215 

false-positive behavior in the presence of selection bias. 216 

217 

The long-term goal of AI-driven antibody discovery is to improve success rate, reduce cost and 218 

development time by shifting key decision-making steps from experimental screening to 219
