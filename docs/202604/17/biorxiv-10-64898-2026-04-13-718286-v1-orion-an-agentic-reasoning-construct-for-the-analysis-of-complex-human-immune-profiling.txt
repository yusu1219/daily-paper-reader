Abstract 21 

The capacity to generate high-dimensional biological datasets has outpaced the ability to 22 interpret them. Technologies such as phage immunoprecipitation and sequencing (PhIP-seq) 23 enable proteome-scale profiling of antibody repertoires, but interpreting thousands of enriched 24 peptides into mechanistic hypotheses remains a labor-intensive bottleneck requiring expert 25 synthesis of statistics, literature, and domain knowledge. Here we describe ORION (Omics 26 Reasoning & Interpretation Orchestrator), a multi-agent framework that uses reasoning-capable 27 large language models to perform end-to-end analysis of complex immune profiling data. 28 ORION integrates statistical analysis, machine learning, and automated literature review into a 29 single structured workflow, producing results that are reproducible and fully traceable. Applied 30 to a published PhIP-seq dataset from autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome type 1 (APS-1), 31 ORION recovered the canonical autoantibody signature in approximately two hours, closely 32 recapitulating an analysis that originally required one to two months of manual effort. To test 33 hypothesis-generation capacity on previously unseen data, we applied ORION to a novel PhIP-34 seq dataset from individuals with Down syndrome, for which no proteome-wide autoantibody 35 reference exists. ORION distinguished disease from control samples with high accuracy, 36 prioritized candidate autoantibody targets, and organized them into biologically coherent groups 37 spanning immune, gut, and neuronal programs, generating testable hypotheses for experimental 38 follow-up. These results demonstrate that agentic AI systems can compress the analysis of 39 complex immune profiling data from weeks to hours, allowing scientists to redirect their time 40 toward the fundamental biology. 41

Introduction 42 The capacity to generate large and increasingly complex biological datasets continues to expand. 43 Core technologies for interrogating biological systems are becoming faster, less expensive, and 44 more capable, broadening the range of questions that can be addressed experimentally and 45 computationally. Sequencing exemplifies this shift: whereas the first human genome required 46 more than a decade and billions of dollars, modern high-throughput platforms can generate a 47 human genome’s worth of data in a day for a few hundred dollars 1,2 . Similar reductions in cost 48 and gains in performance across genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and additional 49 modalities have driven a “multi-omics” era in which complementary, high-dimensional datasets 50 are integrated to interrogate biological systems more comprehensively. In parallel, emerging 51 approaches continue to expand what constitutes an “omic” dataset by enabling systematic 52 measurement of biological processes that were previously difficult to assay at scale. 53 Phage immunoprecipitation and sequencing (PhIP-seq) is one such approach, enabling unbiased, 54 proteome-scale profiling of antibody-antigen interactions 3–7 . By testing antibody reactivity 55 against essentially all human proteins at high throughput, PhIP-seq has helped catalyze 56 “autoreactomics”: the systematic study of autoantibody repertoires in health and disease 8.57 However, unlike genomic, transcriptomic, or proteomic assays, PhIP-seq enrichment counts do 58 not reflect simple molecular abundance; instead, they report antibody-peptide binding behavior 59 shaped by affinity and avidity, epitope features, and immune history. 60 These properties impose distinct analytical challenges that are not readily addressed by standard 61 “omics” pipelines. Autoreactivity is highly individualized, with no clear analogs to 62 transcriptomic housekeeping genes or conserved baseline expression patterns. As a result, 63 identifying biologically meaningful deviations—signals that rise above this background of 64 individuality and may reflect shared autoimmune pathology—requires careful statistical 65 modeling and context-aware interpretation. Individual experiments routinely yield thousands to 66 tens of thousands of enriched peptides, and translating these enrichments into mechanistic 67 hypotheses is time-consuming, often requiring expert synthesis of the literature and relevant 68 databases. 69 Reasoning-capable large language models, such as GPT-5.2 and GPT-5.2 pro, have the potential 70 to alleviate this bottleneck by automating early-stage statistical triage, machine-learning-based 71 pattern finding, and literature/database review for high-dimensional data such as PhIP-seq. When 72 integrated with an agentic reasoning pipeline and code execution environment, these systems 73 prioritize antigenic targets, surface plausible biological connections, and generate testable 74 hypotheses within hours. Here, we describe ORION, a multi-agent framework for analysis of 75 ‘omic data. We present results with previously published data, as well as new data not available 76 to the underlying model. These results suggest that as datasets grow and the atlas of human 77 autoimmune disease profiles is populated, such AI-assisted analysis may enable faster, more 78 consistent interpretation of new patient data and support increasingly nuanced, potentially 79 predictive inference. 80

Methods 90 ORION is a multi-agent framework for end-to-end PhIP-seq analysis organized around persisted 91 intermediate outputs, auditable execution records, and iterative review (Figure 1). Starting from 92 raw input data, it materializes a structured database, extracts cohort-aware features, runs 93 supervised machine learning to prioritize candidate peptides and proteins, and retrieves 94 supporting literature evidence – all orchestrated through a constrained agent loop that enforces 95 reproducibility. All computational steps execute in a sandboxed Python environment. 96 Importantly, ORION is a generalizable framework, appropriate for a large range of biologic 97 ‘omics data. It preserves the core framework architecture and execution model described here. 98 The complete ORION code and schema are available at github.com/openai/orion-multistep-99 analysis. 100

Dataset Materialization. Input matrices are converted into a sparse SQLite schema to reduce 107 storage and accelerate feature extraction. At minimum, the database contains: samples (canonical 108 sample IDs and labels), peptides (peptide identifiers), and rpk_sparse (only nonzero entries, 109 sample × peptide → value). No statistical normalization is applied during import. 110 

Candidate Prioritization. ORION generates candidate peptide and protein lists using three 111 complementary approaches: 112 

• Cohort-aware peptide summaries derived from SQLite queries (prevalence, nonzero 113 abundance summaries, and label-stratified contrasts). 114 

• Supervised feature attribution via logistic regression (D vs HC; details below). 115 

• Protein-level aggregation, when a peptide → protein mapping is available. 116 Prioritized candidates are subsequently passed to the Literature Agent for evidence retrieval and 117 biological contextualization (see Agent Pipeline and Prompting). 118 

Logistic Regression Module. Only D and HC samples are used (minimum 2 per class); AG and 119 any other labels are excluded. A dense sample × peptide matrix is constructed from rpk_sparse 120 with missing pairs filled as 0.0, and peptides are filtered out if all labeled samples have RPK < 121 5.0 (pseudocounts=False); constant features (nunique ≤ 1) are also dropped, and no log transform 122 or scaling is applied. A scikit-learn 9 LogisticRegression model with L2 regularization (C = 0.1, 123 solver = lbfgs, max_iter = 1000) is fit using StratifiedKFold cross-validation with n_folds = 124 min(5, n_per_class). Coefficients are averaged across folds per peptide (mean_coef) and ranked 125 by abs_coef = |mean_coef|, where the sign of mean_coef indicates enrichment direction. When a 126 peptide → protein mapping is available, protein-level scores are computed as 127 aggregate_abs_coef = Σ |mean_coef_peptide| and sum_coef = Σ mean_coef_peptide across all 128 mapped peptides. By default, the top_k = 25 peptides and proteins are reported with full 129 coefficient summaries. 130

Agent Pipeline and Prompting. ORION orchestrates the above steps through three 131 specialized agents operating in a constrained loop in which each stage produces persisted 132 intermediate outputs and execution logs that make the analysis traceable and auditable. 133 Each agent is governed by a structured system prompt that defines its role, required 134 outputs, and operational constraints (full prompt texts are provided in Supplementary 135 Materials). 136

Main Agent (Analysis Agent): The prompt specifies a mandatory four-phase workflow: 137 data sanity and setup, broad-scan differential analysis across ≥100 proteins/peptides, 138 multi-method quantitative inference, and mechanistic interpretation including competing 139 disease models. The prompt enforces AG-bead normalization consideration, prevalence-140 based framing of results, dual peptide- and protein-level reporting, and traceability via 141 persisted SQLite output tables. It prohibits the agent from fabricating external knowledge 142 and requires all external facts to be requested through the Literature Agent interface. 143

Literature Agent: The prompt constrains the agent to return a single structured JSON 144 object containing protein-level annotations (function, subcellular localization, tissue 145 expression, pathway membership, disease associations, and immune-accessibility 146 implications), cluster-level synthesis across multiple query proteins, null-evidence 147 reporting when data are sparse, and scored relevance heuristics. It prioritizes primary 148 databases (PubMed, UniProt, OMIM, Human Protein Atlas) over secondary sources and 149 instructs the agent to focus on normal protein biology rather than restricting searches to 150 autoantibody-specific literature. 151  

> •

Supervisor Agent: The prompt implements a nine-item checklist (data sanity, AG 152 normalization, multi-method analysis, broad-scan coverage, justified narrowing, 153 mechanistic interpretation, competing models, literature compliance, and traceability) 154 with explicit loop-prevention rules—if the Analysis Agent fails to satisfy an item for the 155 same reason twice, it is marked as constrained and the supervisor proceeds rather than re-156 requesting. Termination requires all mandatory checklist items to be satisfied. 157 This Plan → Execute → Verify loop ensures that analysis steps are reproducible and that 158 conclusions are traceable to specific computational outputs. 159

Results 161 

APS-1 benchmark recapitulates a known autoreactive signature 162 

We evaluated ORION on a published PhIP-seq dataset from autoimmune polyendocrine 163 syndrome type 1 (APS-1), a monogenic disorder with a well-characterized autoantibody 164 repertoire 4. Starting from raw enrichment matrices, ORION executed artifact-aware 165 preprocessing, candidate prioritization, and literature-integrated interpretation. The framework 166 recovered the canonical APS-1 autoreactive signature: anti-cytokine antibodies (IL-17F, IL-22, 167 and multiple IFN-α subtypes), steroidogenic enzyme autoantigens (CYP11A1; 52.2% disease vs. 168 0% controls), and organ-specific endotype markers including RFX6 (gastrointestinal), NLRP5 169 (parathyroid), SOX10 (melanocyte), and KHDC3L (ovarian insufficiency). A composite 170 signature score across these targets separated disease from non-disease samples. Overall, these 171 results closely recapitulated the published manually annotation analysis. Total runtime was 2 172 hours and 16 minutes. In contrast, the original manual analysis consumed approximately 1 to 2 173 months, conservatively. 174 

ORION identifies a PhIP-seq signature in Down syndrome after artifact-aware 175 

normalization 176 

In the previous example, the dataset that was used as input to ORION was previously published, 177 and thus available to the underlying reasoning model. To assess the utility of ORION for a 178 dataset that has never been publicly disclosed, and for which no previous immunological 179 signature has been described, we tasked ORION to analyze a PhIP-seq dataset derived from 105 180 individuals with Down syndrome (DS; trisomy 21), 103 age-matched healthy controls (HC), and 181 17 bead-only controls (AG), a control for non-specific phage binding to Protein A/G beads. The 182 dataset comprised 563,322 peptides mapping to 19,949 proteins and was highly sparse (≈86% 183 zeros). ORION completed the full analysis in 1 hour and 41 minutes. 184

Discussion 225 

ORION has immediate implications for the analysis of complex immune profiling data because it 226 converts an often bespoke, expert-driven workflow explorations into a reproducible end-to-end 227 process: cohort-aware statistical triage, supervised feature attribution, and literature-informed 228 biological synthesis that yields explicit, tractable follow-up experiments. Run logs of the ORION 229 Plan → Execute → Verify loop provide transparent and traceable records of each run. Unlike 230 other high dimensionality biological data, like single cell sequencing, PhIP-seq is a stringent test 231 case because its measurements are not simple abundance readouts; enrichment reflects antibody– 232 peptide binding shaped by affinity/avidity, epitope features, and immune history. Moreover, 233 unlike other ‘omics that benefit from shared reference features (e.g., housekeeping genes), each 234 individual’s autoreactive repertoire is highly idiosyncratic, producing variable baselines. As a 235 result, interpretation typically requires context-aware analysis coupled to substantial domain 236 knowledge. ORION’s core innovation is to effectively integrate these steps into a single 237 structured loop that can be applied consistently across large datasets, rather than relying on ad 238 

hoc analyses that vary by analyst and are constrained by time available. Although PhIP-seq is the 239 demonstration modality used here, ORION is designed to be a generalizable framework that can 240 be adapted to a broader range of ‘omic-driven biological data analysis. 241 A key practical consequence is efficiency. While statistical feature discovery can be automated, 242 also integrating a fragmented literature into a coherent mechanistic model and a concrete 243 experimental plan requires training and expert biological intuition. ORION compresses this end-244 to-end cycle so interpretation can plausibly keep pace with data generation, making large 245 retrospective backlogs tractable for hypothesis generation, which in turn can be audited, 246 reviewed, and refined by researchers before being put into action. In APS-1, where the 247 autoantibody landscape is well defined, ORION recovered the expected autoreactivity signature 248 and reproduced canonical separation from healthy controls, validating the end-to-end workflow. 249 However, as noted above, the underlying model was exposed to APS-1 publications, and thus 250 performance should be interpreted as evidence of coherent execution rather than de novo 251 biological discovery. 252 The Down syndrome analysis is more informative as a hypothesis-generation test, because no 253 proteome-wide autoantibody or PhIP-seq reference dataset exists in the literature, and the data 254 used here are among the first of their kind. The results support a heterogeneous, interferon-aligned autoreactivity pattern 258 consistent with trisomy-21 immune remodeling and the interferonopathy-like baseline state 259 described in prior studies 10–13 . DS–HC separation was not driven by a small, highly shared 260 autoantigen class; instead, it emerged from a distributed signature consistent with partially 261 overlapping autoreactivity programs across individuals. Indeed, ORION’s classification task 262 achieved an AUC of 0.91, which is remarkable for any human immune profiling-based 263 assessment. Within this global context, ORION prioritized a smaller set of subset-enriched, 264 immune-accessible candidates and organized them into plausible tissue-linked hypotheses— 265 including barrier-associated and neuro-adhesion–associated programs—while preserving a clear 266 boundary between statistical association and mechanistic inference. Together, this ranking, 267 synthesis, and explicit treatment of uncertainty is intended to shorten the path from large lists of 268 enriched peptides to a small number of coherent, discriminable models suitable for direct testing. 269 We also note the potential clinical relevance of applying ORION to pediatric immune-mediated 270 disease. Down syndrome is among the most common genetic conditions associated with immune 271 dysregulation, affects more than 200,000 individuals in the United States 14 , and confers 272 substantially increased risk of severe infection, autoimmunity, and other immune-related 273 complications beginning in childhood 15,16 . Yet the mechanisms underlying this altered immune 274 state remain only partially defined. In this context, ORION’s ability to identify candidate 275 autoreactive targets and organize them into coherent biological programs may be particularly 276 valuable for pediatric disorders, where mechanistic insight is often limited despite substantial 277 clinical burden. In the present dataset, for example, ORION prioritized autoantibodies targeting 278 IL17F, a cytokine involved in mucosal immunity and host defense that lies within a 279 therapeutically actionable immune signaling axis. If follow-up studies confirm that these 280 antibodies are functionally relevant, such findings could clarify one contributor to immune 281 dysregulation in Down syndrome and help nominate more precise therapeutic strategies. More 282 broadly, many pediatric diseases with suspected immune dysregulation remain mechanistically 283 unresolved despite the existence of high-dimensional immune-profiling data. By accelerating 284 interpretation of such datasets, ORION may help convert clinically important but analytically 285 underused data into experimentally tractable hypotheses across a wider range of pediatric 286 disorders. 287 ORION also has important limitations. Reasoning-capable systems can produce explanations that 288 are plausible but wrong, so interpretation must remain tightly grounded in the pipeline’s 289 statistical outputs and treated as provisional until experimentally validated. As with any 290 hypotheses, follow up suggestions will require scrutiny and refinement. Deployment also faces 291 practical constraints: compute requirements and per-query cost can be substantial, especially for 292 large cohorts and iterative exploration, and should be managed through configurable model 293 selection, iteration limits, and adjustable analysis depth, with local execution options where 294 appropriate. Finally, although PhIP-seq is a demanding demonstration, the broader goal is 295 modality-agnostic. ORION is designed to extend to other ‘omics with appropriate adaptations to 296 input schemas, feature extraction, and prompting, including proteomic and transcriptomic 297 measurements and T cell/B cell repertoire sequencing. 298

The 302 central innovation is that ORION converts high-dimensional, unfamiliar immune profiling data 303 into coherent, testable models at a speed and scale no human team can match, while preserving 304 explicit uncertainty and auditability. These results suggest that early bottlenecks in data analysis 305 of complex human ‘omics data can be rapidly overcome, shifting the bulk of expert scientist time 306 toward fundamental biology: experimental design, experimental execution, model building, and 307 hypothesis testing. 308 309
